The algebra of functions involves combining two functions by addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division to produce new functions. These operations allow us to construct more complex functions from simpler ones.

### 1.8.1 Addition and Subtraction of Functions

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1n0zN51mT75HyOQVlKZ8TJd-EynxUjklk" alt="Addition and subtraction of functions f and g" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 1.8.2:</b> Graphs illustrating addition and subtraction of functions \(f\) and \(g\)</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**  
Given two functions \( f: A \to \mathbb{R} \) and \( g: A \to \mathbb{R} \) defined on the same domain \( A \), the **addition** and **subtraction** of functions are defined by:  
- **Addition:**  
  $$
  (f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x), \quad \forall x \in A
  $$
- **Subtraction:**  
  $$
  (f - g)(x) = f(x) - g(x), \quad \forall x \in A
  $$

**Geometric Meaning:**  
On the graph, the function \( f+g \) at each point \(x\) is the vertical sum of the \( y \)-coordinates of \( f \) and \( g \). Similarly, \( f-g \) is the vertical difference between the values of \( f \) and \( g \) at each \(x\). This means the graph of \(f+g\) can be visualized by adding heights of the graphs of \( f \) and \( g \) pointwise; similarly for subtraction.

---

**Theorem 1 (Properties of Addition and Subtraction of Functions):**  
\[
\boxed{
\begin{cases}
(f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x),\\[6pt]
(f-g)(x) = f(x) - g(x).
\end{cases}
}
\]

**Proof:**  
**Step 1:** Let \(x \in A\) be any point in the domain of both \(f\) and \(g\).  
**Step 2:** By definition of functions, \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are real numbers for each such \(x\).  
**Step 3:** The sum \(f(x) + g(x)\) and difference \(f(x) - g(x)\) are well-defined real numbers since the real numbers are closed under addition and subtraction.  
**Step 4:** Defining the functions \(f+g\) and \(f-g\) by assigning these values to each \(x \in A\) produces functions with domain \(A\).  

Therefore, the definitions hold for all \(x \in A\).

---

### Working Rule (Algorithm) to Add or Subtract Functions:

1. **Check the domains:** Ensure \(f\) and \(g\) have the same domain or consider their common domain \(A\).  
2. **Evaluate each function:** Determine the formulas or expressions for \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\).  
3. **Add/Subtract expressions:** Write \( (f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x) \) or \( (f-g)(x) = f(x) - g(x) \).  
4. **Simplify:** Simplify the resulting expression if possible.  
5. **State domain:** The domain of \(f \pm g\) is \(A\).

---

### Examples

**Example 1 (Basic):**  
Given \( f(x) = 2x + 3 \) and \( g(x) = x^2 - 1 \), find \( (f+g)(x) \) and \( (f-g)(x) \).

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Compute \( (f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x) = (2x + 3) + (x^2 -1) = x^2 + 2x + 2 \).  
Step 2: Compute \( (f-g)(x) = f(x) - g(x) = (2x + 3) - (x^2 -1) = -x^2 + 2x + 4 \).

---

**Example 2 (Conceptual):**  
If \( f(x) = \sin x \) and \( g(x) = \cos x \), find \( (f+g)(\pi/4) \) and interpret geometrically.

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Evaluate \( (f+g)(\pi/4) = \sin (\pi/4) + \cos (\pi/4) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = \sqrt{2} \).  
Step 2: Geometrically, this value represents the sum of the heights of the sine and cosine functions at \( x = \pi/4 \).

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):**  
Given \( f(x) = \sqrt{x} \) and \( g(x) = 3 - x \), find \( (f+g)(x) \) and determine its domain.

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Write \( (f+g)(x) = \sqrt{x} + 3 - x \).  
Step 2: Domain of \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) requires \( x \geq 0 \).  
Step 3: Domain of \(g(x) = 3 - x\) is all real numbers \(\mathbb{R}\).  
Step 4: The common domain is \(x \geq 0\). Hence, the domain of \( (f+g)(x) \) is \([0, \infty)\).

---

### 1.8.2 Multiplication of Functions

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1-NhUPf7rHb7fTCyzmID584nWMzHSVJAo" alt="Multiplication of functions f and g" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 1.8.3:</b> Graph of the product \( f \cdot g \), combining the y-values of \(f\) and \(g\)</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**  
For functions \( f,g : A \to \mathbb{R} \), **multiplication** is defined as:  
$$
(f \cdot g)(x) = f(x) \times g(x), \quad \forall x \in A
$$

**Geometric Meaning:**  
The graph of \(f \cdot g\) at each \(x\) is obtained by multiplying heights \( f(x) \) and \( g(x) \). This operation can produce more complex transformations based on the pointwise products of the functions.

---

**Theorem 2 (Property of Multiplication of Functions):**  
\[
\boxed{
(f \cdot g)(x) = f(x) \times g(x)
}
\]

**Proof:**  
**Step 1:** Let \( x \in A \) be any point in the domain. Since \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are real numbers by definition,  
**Step 2:** Their product \( f(x) \times g(x) \) is also a real number because the reals are closed under multiplication.  
**Step 3:** Defining the function \( f \cdot g \) by assigning \( f(x)g(x) \) to each \( x \in A \) results in a function with domain \( A \).

This establishes the property.

---

### Working Rule (Algorithm) to Multiply Functions:

1. Determine the common domain \(A\) of \(f\) and \(g\).  
2. Write the product function as \( (f \cdot g)(x) = f(x) \times g(x) \).  
3. Multiply the algebraic expressions for each \(x\).  
4. Simplify the expression if possible.  
5. The domain of \(f \cdot g\) is \(A\).

---

### Examples

**Example 1 (Basic):**  
Given \( f(x) = x+2 \) and \( g(x) = 3x - 1 \), find \( (f \cdot g)(x) \).

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Write \( (f \cdot g)(x) = (x + 2)(3x -1) \).  
Step 2: Expand the product:  
\[
(x + 2)(3x -1) = 3x^2 - x + 6x - 2 = 3x^2 + 5x - 2.
\]

---

**Example 2 (Conceptual):**  
If \( f(x) = e^x \) and \( g(x) = \sin x \), find \( (f \cdot g)(0) \).

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Evaluate \( (f \cdot g)(0) = e^0 \times \sin 0 = 1 \times 0 = 0 \).

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):**  
Given \( f(x) = x \) and \( g(x) = \dfrac{1}{x} \), determine \( (f \cdot g)(x) \) and specify its domain.

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Compute \( (f \cdot g)(x) = x \times \frac{1}{x} = 1 \) for all \( x \neq 0 \).  
Step 2: Domain of \(f\) is \(\mathbb{R}\), domain of \(g\) is \(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}\).  
Step 3: The common domain is \(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}\).  
Step 4: Hence, \( (f \cdot g)(x) = 1 \) for every \( x \neq 0 \).

---

### 1.8.3 Division of Functions (where defined)

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1XchMS8AdPldz9dczfnF8s23hp-q4ilsT" alt="Division of functions f over g" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 1.8.4:</b> Graph of quotient \( f/g \) showing points of discontinuity where \( g(x) = 0 \)</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**  
Given two functions \( f, g: A \to \mathbb{R} \), the **division** \( f/g \) is defined by:  
$$
\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}, \quad \forall x \in A \text{ such that } g(x) \neq 0
$$

**Geometric Meaning:**  
The graph of \( f/g \) is obtained by dividing the height of \( f \) by the height of \( g \) at each \( x \) where \( g(x) \neq 0 \). Points where \( g(x) = 0 \) correspond to discontinuities or are undefined for \( f/g \).

---

**Theorem 3 (Property of Division of Functions):**  
\[
\boxed{
\forall x \in A,\quad g(x) \ne 0 \implies \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}
}
\]

**Proof:**  
**Step 1:** Let \( x \in A \) with \( g(x) \neq 0 \). Then the quotient \( \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \) is defined in \(\mathbb{R}\).  
**Step 2:** Define the function \( f/g : \{ x \in A \mid g(x) \neq 0 \} \to \mathbb{R} \) by assigning \( \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \) to each such \(x\).  
**Step 3:** This defines a function whose domain is the subset of \(A\) where \(g(x) \neq 0\), as required.

---

### Working Rule (Algorithm) for Division of Functions:

1. Determine domains of \( f \) and \( g \).  
2. Define domain of \( f/g \) as all \( x \in A \) with \( g(x) \neq 0 \).  
3. Write \( (f/g)(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \).  
4. Simplify the expression for \( (f/g)(x) \) if possible.  
5. Exclude points where \( g(x) = 0 \) from the domain.

---

### Examples

**Example 1 (Basic):**  
Given \( f(x) = x^2 - 1 \) and \( g(x) = x-1 \), find \( \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) \) and determine its domain.

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Write  
\[
\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1}.
\]  
Step 2: For \( x \neq 1 \), simplify the fraction:  
\[
\frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x + 1.
\]  
Step 3: The domain excludes \( x=1 \) because the denominator is zero there. Hence, the domain is all real numbers except 1.

---

**Example 2 (Conceptual):**  
For \( f(x) = \sin x \) and \( g(x) = x \), find \( (f/g)(x) \) and its domain.

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Write  
\[
(f/g)(x) = \frac{\sin x}{x}.
\]  
Step 2: Note \( g(x) = x \neq 0 \) only when \( x \neq 0 \). Thus, the domain is \(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}\).

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):**  
Given \( f(x) = \sqrt{x+3} \) and \( g(x) = x - 1 \), find \( (f/g)(x) \) and its domain.

**Solution:**  
Step 1: Write  
\[
(f/g)(x) = \frac{\sqrt{x+3}}{x-1}.
\]  
Step 2: Determine domain restrictions:  
- \( \sqrt{x+3} \) is defined when \( x + 3 \geq 0 \), i.e., \( x \geq -3 \).  
- The denominator \( x - 1 \neq 0 \implies x \neq 1 \).  
Step 3: The domain is  
\[
[-3, \infty) \setminus \{1\}.
\]

---

## Summary of Formulas for Algebra of Functions:

$$
\begin{cases}
(f + g)(x) = f(x) + g(x), \\[6pt]
(f - g)(x) = f(x) - g(x), \\[6pt]
(f \cdot g)(x) = f(x) \times g(x), \\[6pt]
\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)(x) = \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}, \quad g(x) \neq 0.
\end{cases}
$$
