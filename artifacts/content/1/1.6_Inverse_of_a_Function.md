### 1.6.1 Definition of Invertible Function

A **function** $f: A \to B$ is said to be **invertible** or **one-to-one and onto** (bijective) if there exists a function $g: B \to A$ such that 

$$
g(f(x)) = x \quad \text{for all } x \in A, \quad \text{and} \quad f(g(y)) = y \quad \text{for all } y \in B.
$$

The function $g$ is called the **inverse function** of $f$ and is denoted by $f^{-1}$. The domains and codomains satisfy:

$$
f: A \to B, \quad f^{-1}: B \to A.
$$

**Geometric Meaning:**  
On the coordinate plane, the graph of the inverse function $f^{-1}$ is the reflection of the graph of $f$ about the line $y = x$. This line acts as a mirror. Thus, if a point $(a,b)$ lies on the graph of $f$, then the point $(b,a)$ lies on the graph of $f^{-1}$. 

---

### 1.6.2 Conditions for Invertibility

<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 1.6.2:</b> The horizontal line test: A function $f$ is one-to-one if every horizontal line intersects the graph of $f$ at most once.</i></figcaption>
</figure>

A function $f: A \to B$ has an inverse if and only if it is **bijective**—that is, it must be both **injective (one-to-one)** and **surjective (onto)**.

- **Injective (One-to-one):** Different elements of $A$ map to distinct elements of $B$. Formally, for any $x_1, x_2 \in A$, 
  $$
  f(x_1) = f(x_2) \implies x_1 = x_2.
  $$
- **Surjective (Onto):** For every $y \in B$, there exists an $x \in A$ such that 
  $$
  f(x) = y.
  $$

**Horizontal Line Test:**  
A graphical method to test injectivity is the horizontal line test: The function $f$ is one-to-one if and only if no horizontal line intersects the graph of $f$ more than once.

---

**Theorem 1.6.1.**  
**A function $f: A \to B$ is invertible if and only if it is a bijection.**

$$
\boxed{
f \text{ is invertible } \iff f \text{ is one-to-one and onto}.
}
$$

**Proof:**

- **(⇒ Direction)** Suppose $f$ is invertible with inverse $f^{-1}$.  
  Since $f^{-1}$ is a function from $B$ to $A$, for every $y \in B$, there is exactly one $x \in A$ such that $f(x) = y$. This implies:  
  - $f$ is injective, because if there existed $x_1 \neq x_2$ with $f(x_1) = f(x_2) = y$, then $f^{-1}(y)$ would not be uniquely defined.  
  - $f$ is surjective, because $f^{-1}$ is defined on all $B$.  

  Therefore, $f$ is bijective.

- **(⇐ Direction)** Suppose $f$ is bijective.  
  By surjectivity, for each $y \in B$, there exists at least one $x \in A$ such that $f(x) = y$. By injectivity, this $x$ is unique. Define 
  $$
  f^{-1}: B \to A \quad \text{by} \quad f^{-1}(y) = x \text{ where } f(x) = y.
  $$
  Then for all $x \in A$ and $y \in B$,
  $$
  f^{-1}(f(x)) = x, \quad f(f^{-1}(y)) = y.
  $$
  Thus, $f^{-1}$ is the inverse function of $f$.

Hence, $f$ is invertible if and only if it is bijective.

---

### 1.6.3 Finding the Inverse Function

To **find the inverse function** $f^{-1}$ of a given invertible function $f$, follow these steps:

**Formulas:**

- If $y = f(x)$ is given, then $x = f^{-1}(y)$.
- Interchange the roles of $x$ and $y$ and solve for $y$ to get $f^{-1}(x)$.

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm) to find $f^{-1}$**

1. **Step 1:** Write the function in the form 
   $$
   y = f(x).
   $$

2. **Step 2:** Interchange $x$ and $y$, i.e., write 
   $$
   x = f(y).
   $$

3. **Step 3:** Solve this equation for $y$ in terms of $x$.

4. **Step 4:** The expression obtained for $y$ is the inverse function 
   $$
   f^{-1}(x) = y.
   $$

5. **Step 5:** Specify the domain and range of $f^{-1}$ carefully (the domain of $f^{-1}$ is the range of $f$, and vice versa).

---

### 1.6.4 Graded Solved Examples

---

**Example 1.6.1 (Concept Building):**  
Find the inverse function of 

$$
f(x) = 3x + 5, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}.
$$

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Write 
$$
y = 3x + 5.
$$

**Step 2:** Interchange $x$ and $y$:

$$
x = 3y + 5.
$$

**Step 3:** Solve for $y$:

$$
3y = x - 5 \implies y = \frac{x - 5}{3}.
$$

**Step 4:** Therefore,

$$
f^{-1}(x) = \frac{x - 5}{3}.
$$

**Step 5:** Domain and range of both $f$ and $f^{-1}$ are $\mathbb{R}$.

---

**Example 1.6.2 (Competency Based):**  
Find the inverse of the function 

$$
f(x) = \frac{2x - 3}{5}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}.
$$

Also, verify that $f(f^{-1}(x)) = x$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Write 

$$
y = \frac{2x - 3}{5}.
$$

**Step 2:** Interchange $x$ and $y$:

$$
x = \frac{2y - 3}{5}.
$$

**Step 3:** Solve for $y$:

Multiply both sides by 5:

$$
5x = 2y - 3,
$$

then 

$$
2y = 5x + 3 \implies y = \frac{5x + 3}{2}.
$$

**Step 4:** So,

$$
f^{-1}(x) = \frac{5x + 3}{2}.
$$

**Step 5:** Verify $f(f^{-1}(x))$:

$$
f\left(f^{-1}(x)\right) = f\left(\frac{5x + 3}{2}\right) = \frac{2 \times \frac{5x + 3}{2} - 3}{5} = \frac{5x + 3 - 3}{5} = \frac{5x}{5} = x.
$$

Hence, verification is complete.

---

**Example 1.6.3 (HOTS):**  
Given that 

$$
f(x) = \frac{3x - 4}{2x + 5},
$$

find $f^{-1}(x)$. Also, find the domain of $f^{-1}$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Write 

$$
y = \frac{3x - 4}{2x + 5}.
$$

**Step 2:** Interchange $x$ and $y$:

$$
x = \frac{3y - 4}{2y + 5}.
$$

**Step 3:** Solve for $y$:

Multiply both sides by $2y + 5$:

$$
x(2y + 5) = 3y - 4,
$$

which expands to

$$
2xy + 5x = 3y - 4.
$$

Rearranged to group $y$ terms:

$$
2xy - 3y = -5x - 4,
$$

$$
y(2x - 3) = -5x - 4,
$$

so

$$
y = \frac{-5x - 4}{2x - 3}.
$$

**Step 4:** Therefore,

$$
f^{-1}(x) = \frac{-5x - 4}{2x - 3}.
$$

**Step 5:** Determine the domain of $f^{-1}$:

Denominator cannot be zero:

$$
2x - 3 \neq 0 \implies x \neq \frac{3}{2}.
$$

Hence,

$$
\mathrm{Domain}(f^{-1}) = \{ x \in \mathbb{R} \mid x \neq \tfrac{3}{2} \}.
$$

---

**Example 1.6.4:**  
Check if the function 

$$
f(x) = x^2, \quad x \in \mathbb{R},
$$

has an inverse.

**Solution:**

- **Step 1:** Check if $f$ is one-to-one.  
Consider $x_1 = 2$ and $x_2 = -2$:

$$
f(2) = 4, \quad f(-2) = 4,
$$

but $2 \neq -2$. Thus, $f$ is not one-to-one on $\mathbb{R}$.

- **Step 2:** Since $f$ is not injective on $\mathbb{R}$, it is not invertible on $\mathbb{R}$.

- **Step 3:** Restrict the domain to $[0, \infty)$:

Then the function

$$
f: [0, \infty) \to [0, \infty)
$$

is one-to-one and onto.

The inverse function on this domain is 

$$
f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{x}.
$$

---

**Important Note:**  
Functions may not be invertible on their entire domains but can be invertible on suitable restricted domains.
