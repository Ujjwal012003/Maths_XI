In trigonometry, the addition and subtraction formulas allow us to express the trigonometric function of the sum or difference of two angles in terms of the individual functions of these angles. When extended to inverse trigonometric functions, these identities become vital tools for simplifying expressions and solving equations involving inverse trigonometric terms. This section delves into the addition and subtraction formulas with a special focus on the **Arctangent Addition Formula**, its proof, usage conditions, and examples, followed by other essential trigonometric identities.

### 2.4.1 Arctangent Addition Formula

#### Introduction

The **arctangent addition formula** expresses the sum of two inverse tangents as a single inverse tangent of a rational expression involving the tangentsâ€™ arguments. It is a powerful tool that simplifies expressions containing sums of \(\tan^{-1}\) terms and aids in solving trigonometric equations.

#### Formal Definition

**Let \( a, b \in \mathbb{R} \) be real numbers. Then, under suitable conditions,**

\[
\tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{a+b}{1 - ab} \right) + k \pi,
\]

**where \( k \) is an integer that depends on the values of \( a \) and \( b \) ensuring the correct principal value of the arctangent.**

#### Geometric Meaning

Recall that \(\tan^{-1}(x)\) is the angle whose tangent is \(x\). On the Cartesian plane, if a point \(P\) has slope \(a\) relative to the \(x\)-axis, and point \(Q\) has slope \(b\), then the tangent of the angle between the \(x\)-axis and the line joining the origin to \(P\) is \(a\), similarly for \(Q\). The **arctangent addition formula** gives the tangent of the sum of these two angles. Graphically, the formula corresponds to the tangent of the sum of two angles constructed from slopes \(a\) and \(b\).

---

#### Theorem 2.4.1.1: Arctangent Addition Formula

\[
\boxed{
\tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = 
\begin{cases}
\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{a+b}{1 - ab}\right), & \text{if } ab < 1, \\[10pt]
\dfrac{\pi}{2}, & \text{if } ab = 1, \\[10pt]
\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{a+b}{1 - ab}\right) + \pi, & \text{if } ab > 1 \text{ and } a, b > 0, \\[10pt]
\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{a+b}{1 - ab}\right) - \pi, & \text{if } ab > 1 \text{ and } \text{either } a < 0 \text{ or } b < 0.
\end{cases}
}
\]

---

#### Step-by-step Proof of Theorem 2.4.1.1

**Step 1: Let**

\[
\alpha = \tan^{-1}(a), \quad \beta = \tan^{-1}(b).
\]

By definition,

\[
\tan \alpha = a, \quad \tan \beta = b,
\]
with \(\alpha, \beta \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)\) (principal branch of \(\tan^{-1}\)).

**Step 2: Use the tangent addition formula for \(\alpha + \beta\):**

\[
\tan(\alpha + \beta) = \frac{\tan \alpha + \tan \beta}{1 - \tan \alpha \tan \beta} = \frac{a + b}{1 - ab}.
\]

**Step 3: Express \(\alpha + \beta\) in terms of \(\tan^{-1}\):**

Since

\[
\tan(\alpha + \beta) = \frac{a + b}{1 - ab},
\]

it follows that

\[
\alpha + \beta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a + b}{1 - ab}\right) + k \pi,
\]

for some integer \(k\) chosen to ensure the angle lies in the correct range.

**Step 4: Determine the integer \(k\) based on the value of \(ab\) and the signs of \(a, b\):**

- If \(ab < 1\), then the argument \(\frac{a+b}{1 - ab}\) lies within the range of the principal branch of \(\tan^{-1}\), so

\[
\tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a + b}{1 - ab}\right).
\]

- If \(ab = 1\), then the denominator is zero, and the tangent function tends to infinity, implying

\[
\tan(\alpha + \beta) \to \pm \infty,
\]

which corresponds to

\[
\alpha + \beta = \pm \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]

Since both \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) lie in \((-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2})\), the sum is

\[
\tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]

- If \(ab > 1\), the value \(\frac{a + b}{1 - ab}\) falls outside the principal range, and an additional \(\pi\) or \(-\pi\) must be added based on the signs of \(a\) and \(b\) to represent the correct angle:

\[
\tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a + b}{1 - ab}\right) \pm \pi.
\]

---

### Important Notes:

- The formula is valid only if the denominator \(1 - ab \neq 0\).
- Correct selection of branches of the inverse tangent function is crucial to obtain the correct sum.
- The formula also applies to subtraction by interpreting subtraction as addition with the negative argument.

#### Fundamental Addition and Subtraction Formulas for Sine and Cosine

The basic addition and subtraction formulas for sine and cosine are:

\[
\sin (x \pm y) = \sin x \cos y \pm \cos x \sin y,
\]

\[
\cos (x \pm y) = \cos x \cos y \mp \sin x \sin y.
\]

These formulas allow angles to be decomposed or combined, crucial for simplifying algebraic and trigonometric expressions.

#### Addition and Subtraction Formulas for Tangent

Similarly, tangent satisfies:

\[
\tan(x \pm y) = \frac{\tan x \pm \tan y}{1 \mp \tan x \tan y}.
\]

### 2.4.3 Working Rules (Algorithm)

**How to use the arctangent addition formula to simplify or solve problems:**

1. **Identify** two angles expressed as \(\tan^{-1}(a)\) and \(\tan^{-1}(b)\) whose sum or difference is to be evaluated.

2. **Calculate** the product \(ab\).

3. **Determine the condition and apply the appropriate formula:**
   - If \(ab < 1\), then
     \[
     \tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a + b}{1 - ab}\right).
     \]
   - If \(ab = 1\), then
     \[
     \tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = \frac{\pi}{2}.
     \]
   - If \(ab > 1\), adjust the final answer by adding or subtracting \(\pi\) according to the signs of \(a\) and \(b\).

4. **Compute** the fraction \(\frac{a + b}{1 - ab}\).

5. **Evaluate** \(\tan^{-1}\) of the computed fraction.

6. **Adjust** the value by \(\pi\) if required to reflect the correct quadrant.

7. **Simplify** and verify the result if possible.

### 2.4.4 Graded Solved Examples

---

#### Example 1 (Concept Building):

**Find the value of**

\[
\tan^{-1}(2) + \tan^{-1}(3).
\]

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate the product \( ab = 2 \times 3 = 6 > 1. \)

**Step 2:** Compute

\[
\frac{a + b}{1 - ab} = \frac{2 + 3}{1 - 6} = \frac{5}{-5} = -1.
\]

**Step 3:** Since \(ab > 1\) and both \(a, b > 0\), apply

\[
\tan^{-1}(2) + \tan^{-1}(3) = \tan^{-1}(-1) + \pi.
\]

**Step 4:** Evaluate \(\tan^{-1}(-1) = -\frac{\pi}{4}\).

**Step 5:** Therefore,

\[
\tan^{-1}(2) + \tan^{-1}(3) = -\frac{\pi}{4} + \pi = \frac{3\pi}{4}.
\]

---

#### Example 2 (Competency-Based):

**Evaluate**

\[
\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) + \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{7}\right).
\]

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Calculate \( ab = \frac{1}{3} \times \frac{1}{7} = \frac{1}{21} < 1. \)

**Step 2:** Find

\[
\frac{a + b}{1 - ab} = \frac{\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{7}}{1 - \frac{1}{21}} = \frac{\frac{7 + 3}{21}}{\frac{20}{21}} = \frac{\frac{10}{21}}{\frac{20}{21}} = \frac{10}{20} = \frac{1}{2}.
\]

**Step 3:** Therefore,

\[
\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) + \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{7}\right) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right).
\]

---

#### Example 3 (HOTS):

**Show that**

\[
\tan^{-1}(1) + \tan^{-1}(2) + \tan^{-1}(3) = \pi.
\]

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** First compute:

\[
A = \tan^{-1}(1) + \tan^{-1}(2).
\]

Calculate \( ab = 1 \times 2 = 2 > 1. \)

Compute

\[
\frac{1 + 2}{1 - 2} = \frac{3}{-1} = -3.
\]

Thus, by the formula,

\[
A = \tan^{-1}(-3) + \pi,
\]

since \(ab > 1\) and \(a, b > 0\).

Recall

\[
\tan^{-1}(-3) = -\tan^{-1}(3),
\]

therefore,

\[
A = -\tan^{-1}(3) + \pi.
\]

**Step 2:** Now add \(\tan^{-1}(3)\):

\[
A + \tan^{-1}(3) = -\tan^{-1}(3) + \pi + \tan^{-1}(3) = \pi.
\]

Hence,

\[
\tan^{-1}(1) + \tan^{-1}(2) + \tan^{-1}(3) = \pi,
\]

as required.

---

#### Example 4:

**Express \(\tan^{-1}(x) - \tan^{-1}(y)\) as a single arctangent.**

**Solution:**

Subtraction is treated as addition with a negative argument:

\[
\tan^{-1}(x) - \tan^{-1}(y) = \tan^{-1}(x) + \tan^{-1}(-y).
\]

Using the arctangent addition formula:

\[
= \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x - y}{1 + xy} \right),
\]

provided \(1 + xy \neq 0.\)

---

### Summary of Important Addition and Subtraction Formulas

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\sin (x \pm y) = \sin x \cos y \pm \cos x \sin y, \\
&\cos (x \pm y) = \cos x \cos y \mp \sin x \sin y, \\
&\tan (x \pm y) = \frac{\tan x \pm \tan y}{1 \mp \tan x \tan y}, \\
&\tan^{-1}(a) + \tan^{-1}(b) = 
\begin{cases}
\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{a+b}{1 - ab}\right), & ab < 1, \\[6pt]
\dfrac{\pi}{2}, & ab = 1, \\[6pt]
\tan^{-1}\left(\dfrac{a+b}{1 - ab}\right) \pm \pi, & ab > 1,
\end{cases}
\end{aligned}
}
\]

where the \(\pm \pi\) sign is chosen based on the signs of \(a\) and \(b\) as explained.
