**
\[
\boxed{
\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{1 - x^2}}.
}
\]

### 2.8.4 Geometric Problems Using Inverse Trig Ratios

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1OHmDcRI_VWta_XyjiNmrEmQK7zCQOQ2J" alt="Using inverse trig ratios to find angles in triangles" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.8.4:</b> Right triangle with labeled sides showing inverse trig ratios to determine angles</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Introduction:**  
Inverse trigonometric functions allow us to find angles given sides of triangles, crucial in coordinate geometry, vector analysis, and physical contexts.

#### Definitions & Geometric Meaning

Given a right triangle with sides \(\mathrm{opposite} = a\), \(\mathrm{adjacent} = b\), and hypotenuse \(c\), the basic ratios are:

\[
\sin \theta = \frac{a}{c}, \quad \cos \theta = \frac{b}{c}, \quad \tan \theta = \frac{a}{b}.
\]

Applying inverse trigonometric functions to known ratios yields the angle \(\theta\):

\[
\theta = \sin^{-1} \frac{a}{c}, \quad \theta = \cos^{-1} \frac{b}{c}, \quad \theta = \tan^{-1} \frac{a}{b}.
\]

Graphically, \(\theta\) corresponds to the angle formed by vector or point \((b,a)\) in the coordinate quadrant.

---

#### Theorem 2.8.4.1 (Inverse Trig Ratios in Right Triangles)

**Boxed:**  
Given the side lengths of a right triangle, the measure of the angle opposite to side \(a\) is
\[
\boxed{
\theta = \sin^{-1} \frac{a}{c}.
}
\]

**Proof:**  
By the Pythagorean theorem,
\[
c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}.
\]
Since sides are positive lengths, the ratio \(\frac{a}{c} \in [0, 1]\), making \(\theta = \sin^{-1} \frac{a}{c}\) uniquely defined in \(\left[0, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]\).

---

#### Working Rule to Solve Geometric Problems

1. Identify given triangle sides or ratios.  
2. Determine the trigonometric ratio from the given data.  
3. Apply the inverse trigonometric function to compute the angle.  
4. Use angle sum properties, Pythagorean theorem, or laws of sines and cosines for other unknowns.  
5. Validate answers, ensuring angles lie within realistic bounds.

---

#### Graded Solved Examples

**Example 1 (Basic):**  
Find the angle opposite side \(5\) in a right triangle with hypotenuse \(13\).

*Solution:*  
**Step 1:** Calculate ratio:
\[
\frac{5}{13}.
\]

**Step 2:** Compute angle:
\[
\theta = \sin^{-1} \frac{5}{13}.
\]

**Step 3:** Numerically,
\[
\theta \approx \sin^{-1}(0.3846) \approx 22.62^\circ.
\]

---

**Example 2 (Intermediate):**  
In a triangle, the adjacent side to angle \(\theta\) is 4 units and the opposite side 3 units. Find \(\theta\).

*Solution:*  
**Step 1:** Compute ratio:
\[
\tan \theta = \frac{3}{4}.
\]

**Step 2:** Apply inverse tangent:
\[
\theta = \tan^{-1} \frac{3}{4}.
\]

**Step 3:** Numerically,
\[
\theta \approx 36.87^\circ.
\]

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):**  
A ladder leaning against a wall forms an angle \(\theta\) with the ground. If the foot of the ladder moves 3 m away from the wall, the angle reduces by \(15^\circ\). Given the ladder length is 10 m, find the original angle \(\theta\).

*Solution:*  
**Step 1:** Let the original angle be \(\theta\). The height on the wall:
\[
h = 10 \sin \theta.
\]
Original base:
\[
b = 10 \cos \theta.
\]

**Step 2:** After moving 3 m away, the new base is
\[
b' = b + 3 = 10 \cos \theta + 3.
\]
The new angle is \(\theta - 15^\circ\).

Height remains the same,
\[
h = 10 \sin \theta = L \sin(\theta - 15^\circ).
\]
Since \(L=10\), using tangents is more useful:

\[
\tan(\theta - 15^\circ) = \frac{h}{b'} = \frac{10 \sin \theta}{10 \cos \theta + 3}.
\]

**Step 3:** Use the tangent difference formula:
\[
\tan(\alpha - \beta) = \frac{\tan \alpha - \tan \beta}{1 + \tan \alpha \tan \beta}.
\]
Let \( t = \tan \theta \) and \(\tan 15^\circ = 2 - \sqrt{3}\). Then,
\[
\frac{t - (2 - \sqrt{3})}{1 + t(2 - \sqrt{3})} = \frac{10 \sin \theta}{10 \cos \theta + 3}.
\]

Express \( \sin \theta \) and \( \cos \theta \) in terms of \(t\),
\[
\sin \theta = \frac{t}{\sqrt{1 + t^2}}, \quad \cos \theta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + t^2}}.
\]

Therefore,
\[
\frac{t - (2 - \sqrt{3})}{1 + t(2 - \sqrt{3})} = \frac{10 \frac{t}{\sqrt{1 + t^2}}}{10 \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + t^2}} + 3} = \frac{10 t}{10 + 3 \sqrt{1 + t^2}}.
\]

**Step 4:** This reduces to an equation in \(t\), which can be solved numerically. The solution yields
\[
t \approx \sqrt{3},
\]
so
\[
\theta = \tan^{-1} t \approx \tan^{-1} \sqrt{3} = 60^\circ.
\]

### 2.8.5 Inequalities Involving Inverse Trigonometric Functions

**Introduction:**  
Inequalities involving inverse trig functions are important in bounding values of angles or expressions that arise in geometry, analysis, and problem-solving.

---

#### Important Inequalities

1. For \( x \in [0,1] \):
\[
x \leq \sin^{-1} x \leq \frac{\pi}{2} x.
\]

2. For \( x \in (0, \infty) \):
\[
0 < \tan^{-1} x < \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]

3. For \( x \in [0,1] \):
\[
\frac{x}{1 + \sqrt{1 - x^2}} \leq \sin^{-1} x \leq \frac{\pi}{2} x.
\]

---

#### Theorem 2.8.5.1 (Monotonicity and Bounds)

**Boxed:**  
The function \( y = \sin^{-1} x \) is increasing and concave on \([0,1]\), and for \( x \in [0,1] \),
\[
\boxed{
x \leq \sin^{-1} x \leq \frac{\pi}{2} x.
}
\]

**Proof (Sketch):**  
- Since
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \geq 1, \quad x \in [0,1),
\]
the function grows faster than the linear function \( y = x \).

- Using Taylor series expansion or geometric arguments, one obtains
\[
\sin^{-1} x \leq \frac{\pi}{2} x.
\]

---

#### Working Rule to Solve Inequalities

1. Identify the relevant range of \(x\).  
2. Use monotonicity and known inequalities of the involved inverse trig functions.  
3. If needed, use integral representations or series expansions to establish bounds.  
4. Apply algebraic manipulations carefully respecting domains.

---

#### Graded Solved Examples

**Example 1 (Basic):**  
Show that for \( x \in [0,1] \),
\[
\sin^{-1} x \geq x.
\]

*Solution:*  
**Step 1:** Observe that \(\sin^{-1} x\) is increasing and tangent to \(y = x\) at the origin.

**Step 2:** Since
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} x \geq 1,
\]
the curve \( y = \sin^{-1} x \) lies above \( y = x \) for \(x > 0\).

Hence,
\[
\sin^{-1} x \geq x.
\]

---

**Example 2 (Intermediate):**  
Prove that for \(x > 0\),
\[
\tan^{-1} x < x.
\]

*Solution:*  
**Step 1:** Define
\[
f(x) = x - \tan^{-1} x.
\]

**Step 2:** Differentiate:
\[
f'(x) = 1 - \frac{1}{1 + x^2} = \frac{x^2}{1 + x^2} > 0 \quad \forall x > 0.
\]

**Step 3:** Since \(f(0) = 0\) and \(f'(x) > 0\), \(f\) is increasing and positive for \(x > 0\).

Hence,
\[
\tan^{-1} x < x.
\]

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):**  
For \( x \in (0,1) \), prove that
\[
\sin^{-1} x < \frac{\pi}{2} x.
\]

*Solution:*  
**Step 1:** Define
\[
g(x) = \frac{\pi}{2} x - \sin^{-1} x.
\]

**Step 2:** Differentiate,
\[
g'(x) = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}.
\]

Since for \(x \in (0,1)\),
\[
\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \geq 1,
\]
and \(\frac{\pi}{2} \approx 1.5708 > 1\), at \(x=0\),
\[
g(0) = 0,
\]
and at \(x=1\),
\[
g(1) = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\pi}{2} = 0.
\]

By concavity or higher order derivative tests (omitted here for brevity), \(g(x) > 0\) on \((0,1)\), proving the inequality.
