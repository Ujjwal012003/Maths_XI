### 2.9.1 Sum and Difference Identities

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Addition of two angles illustrated on unit circle" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.9.1:</b> Visualization of sum of two angles on the unit circle</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Introduction:**  
The sum and difference identities help express trigonometric functions of angle sums or differences in terms of functions of individual angles. Geometrically, these identities correspond to combining rotations by angles \( A \) and \( B \) around the origin on the unit circle.

---

**Definitions and Geometric Meaning:**  
For two angles \( A \) and \( B \), the **sum identities** express \( \sin(A+B) \) and \( \cos(A+B) \), and the **difference identities** express \( \sin(A - B) \) and \( \cos(A - B) \) in terms of \( \sin A, \cos A, \sin B, \cos B \).

On the unit circle, adding an angle corresponds to rotating the point by the second angle. The coordinates of the rotated point give the sine and cosine values of the sum.

---

**Theorems:**

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\sin(A + B) &= \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B \\
\sin(A - B) &= \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B \\
\cos(A + B) &= \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B \\
\cos(A - B) &= \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B
\end{aligned}
}
\]

---

**Proof of \( \sin(A + B) \) Identity:**

**Step 1:** Consider the point \( P = (\cos A, \sin A) \) on the unit circle representing angle \( A \). Rotating \( P \) by an angle \( B \) results in a point \( Q \) whose coordinates correspond to the sine and cosine of \( A+B \).

**Step 2:** The rotation matrix for an angle \( B \) is:
\[
R_B = \begin{bmatrix} \cos B & -\sin B \\ \sin B & \cos B \end{bmatrix}
\]
Applying \( R_B \) to \( P \):
\[
Q = R_B P = \begin{bmatrix} \cos B & -\sin B \\ \sin B & \cos B \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos A \\ \sin A \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos B \cos A - \sin B \sin A \\ \sin B \cos A + \cos B \sin A \end{bmatrix}
\]

**Step 3:** The \( y \)-coordinate of \( Q \) is \( \sin(A + B) \), so:
\[
\sin(A + B) = \sin B \cos A + \cos B \sin A = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B
\]
(reordering terms since addition is commutative).

---

**Proof of \( \cos(A + B) \) Identity:**

Using the \( x \)-coordinate of \( Q \):
\[
\cos(A + B) = \cos B \cos A - \sin B \sin A = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B
\]

---

**Proofs of Difference Identities:**

Use the relation \( \sin(A - B) = \sin(A + (-B)) \) and the even-odd properties of sine and cosine:  
- \( \sin(-B) = -\sin B \)  
- \( \cos(-B) = \cos B \)

Substitute into sum formulas,
\[
\sin(A - B) = \sin A \cos B - \cos A \sin B, \quad \cos(A - B) = \cos A \cos B + \sin A \sin B.
\]

---

**Formulas:**

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sin(A \pm B) &= \sin A \cos B \pm \cos A \sin B \\
\cos(A \pm B) &= \cos A \cos B \mp \sin A \sin B
\end{aligned}
\]

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm for sum/difference identities):**

1. Identify the given expression involving sum/difference angles.
2. Replace functions like \( \sin(A \pm B) \) or \( \cos(A \pm B) \) by using above formulas directly.
3. Evaluate or simplify using known values or expressions for \( \sin A, \cos A, \sin B, \cos B \).
4. Check for special cases like \( A = B \), or when denominator terms zero (for tangents, etc.).

---

**Graded Solved Examples:**

**Type 1 (Concept Building):**  
Find \( \sin 75^\circ \) using sum identity.

*Solution:*  
Step 1: Write \( 75^\circ = 45^\circ + 30^\circ \).  
Step 2: Use formula:
\[
\sin(75^\circ) = \sin 45^\circ \cos 30^\circ + \cos 45^\circ \sin 30^\circ
\]  
Step 3: Substitute \(\sin 45^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \cos 30^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, \cos 45^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \sin 30^\circ = \frac{1}{2}\).  
Step 4: Calculate:
\[
\sin 75^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{4} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{4} = \frac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}}{4}.
\]

---

**Type 2 (Competency Based):**  
Prove that  
\[
\sin 3\theta = 3 \sin \theta - 4 \sin^3 \theta
\]
using sum and difference formulas.

*Solution:*  
Step 1: Write \( 3\theta = 2\theta + \theta \).  
Step 2: Use sum formula:
\[
\sin(3\theta) = \sin(2\theta + \theta) = \sin 2\theta \cos \theta + \cos 2\theta \sin \theta.
\]  
Step 3: Recall double angle formulas:
\[
\sin 2\theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta, \quad \cos 2\theta = 1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta.
\]  
Step 4: Substitute:
\[
\sin(3\theta) = (2 \sin \theta \cos \theta) \cos \theta + (1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta) \sin \theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos^2 \theta + \sin \theta - 2 \sin^3 \theta.
\]  
Step 5: Use Pythagorean identity \( \cos^2 \theta = 1 - \sin^2 \theta \):
\[
2 \sin \theta (1 - \sin^2 \theta) + \sin \theta - 2 \sin^3 \theta = 2 \sin \theta - 2 \sin^3 \theta + \sin \theta - 2 \sin^3 \theta = 3 \sin \theta - 4 \sin^3 \theta.
\]

---

**Type 3 (HOTS):**  
Find the exact value of:
\[
\sin 15^\circ + \sin 75^\circ.
\]

*Solution:*  
Step 1: Use the sum-to-product identity:
\[
\sin x + \sin y = 2 \sin \frac{x + y}{2} \cos \frac{x - y}{2}.
\]  
Step 2: Calculate:
\[
x = 15^\circ, \quad y = 75^\circ, \quad \Rightarrow \frac{x + y}{2} = 45^\circ, \quad \frac{x - y}{2} = -30^\circ.
\]  
Step 3:
\[
\sin 15^\circ + \sin 75^\circ = 2 \sin 45^\circ \cos (-30^\circ) = 2 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \sqrt{2} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}.
\]

### 2.9.2 Multiple Angle Formulas

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Graphs illustrating sine of multiple angles" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.9.2:</b> Graphical behavior of sine function for multiple angles</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Introduction:**  
Multiple angle formulas express trigonometric functions of multiples of an angle in terms of powers or polynomials of trigonometric functions of the original angle. Graphically, they capture how oscillations speed up by multiplying the angle.

---

**Definition and Geometric Meaning:**  
For an angle \( \theta \), the multiple angle formulas like \( \sin 2\theta, \sin 3\theta \), etc., give insights into how the function behaves under angular multiplication — this reflects stretching along the angular axis on the unit circle.

---

**Theorems (Key Multiple Angle Formulas):**

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\sin 2\theta &= 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta \\
\sin 3\theta &= 3 \sin \theta - 4 \sin^3 \theta \\
\cos 2\theta &= \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta = 2 \cos^2 \theta - 1 = 1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta \\
\cos 3\theta &= 4 \cos^3 \theta - 3 \cos \theta
\end{aligned}
}
\]

---

**Proof of \( \sin 2\theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta \):**

Use the sum formula with \( A = B = \theta \):
\[
\sin 2\theta = \sin(\theta + \theta) = \sin \theta \cos \theta + \cos \theta \sin \theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta.
\]

---

**Proof of \( \sin 3\theta \):**

Write \( 3 \theta = 2 \theta + \theta \) and apply sum formula:
\[
\sin 3\theta = \sin(2\theta + \theta) = \sin 2\theta \cos \theta + \cos 2\theta \sin \theta.
\]
Using identities for \( \sin 2\theta \) and \( \cos 2\theta \) as above, proceed as in the previous example to obtain:
\[
\sin 3\theta = 3 \sin \theta - 4 \sin^3 \theta.
\]

---

**Formulas:**

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sin n\theta &= \text{Expressible as sums/powers of } \sin \theta, \cos \theta \\
\cos n\theta &= \text{Likewise polynomial in } \cos \theta \text{ and } \sin \theta
\end{aligned}
\]

Specific formulas for \( n=2,3 \) are given above.

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm):**

1. Express \( n\theta \) as a sum of smaller angles, e.g., \( 2\theta, 3\theta \).  
2. Apply the sum formula repeatedly to reduce expressions to powers/products of \( \sin \theta \) and \( \cos \theta \).  
3. Use Pythagorean identities when required to simplify powers, e.g., \( \cos^2 \theta = 1 - \sin^2 \theta \).   
4. Factor to gain compact expressions if possible.

---

**Graded Solved Examples:**

**Type 1:**  
Show that \( \cos 2\theta = 1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta \).

*Solution:*  
Step 1: Recall the double angle formula:
\[
\cos 2\theta = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta.
\]  
Step 2: Substitute \( \cos^2 \theta = 1 - \sin^2 \theta \):
\[
\cos 2\theta = (1 - \sin^2 \theta) - \sin^2 \theta = 1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta.
\]

---

**Type 2:**  
Prove:
\[
\sin 4\theta = 4 \sin \theta \cos^3 \theta - 4 \sin^3 \theta \cos \theta.
\]

*Solution:*  
Step 1: Write \( 4\theta = 2(2\theta) \).  
Step 2: Use the double angle formula with \( \alpha = 2\theta \):
\[
\sin 4\theta = 2 \sin 2\theta \cos 2\theta.
\]  
Step 3: Substitute known formulas:
\[
\sin 2\theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta, \quad \cos 2\theta = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta.
\]  
Step 4: Thus:
\[
\sin 4\theta = 2 \cdot (2 \sin \theta \cos \theta) \cdot (\cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta) = 4 \sin \theta \cos \theta (\cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta).
\]  
Step 5: Distribute:
\[
4 \sin \theta \cos^3 \theta - 4 \sin^3 \theta \cos \theta.
\]

---

**Type 3 (HOTS):**  
Express \( \cos 5\theta \) in terms of powers of \( \cos \theta \).

*Solution Outline:*  
Use the recursive formula or De Moivre’s theorem:
\[
\cos n \theta = 2 \cos \theta \cos ((n-1) \theta) - \cos ((n-2) \theta),
\]
applying repeatedly or use binomial expansion on \( (\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)^5 \). The explicit formula is:
\[
\cos 5\theta = 16 \cos^5 \theta - 20 \cos^3 \theta + 5 \cos \theta.
\]

### 2.9.3 Combining Inverse Trigonometric Functions

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Graphs showing inverse trigonometric functions and their ranges" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.9.3:</b> Graphs of inverse sine and cosine functions showing principal branches</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Introduction:**  
Inverse trigonometric functions allow the determination of angles from function values. Combining these functions with algebraic operations involves careful use of domain and range considerations and sometimes relies on trigonometric identities for simplification.

---

**Definition & Geometric Meaning:**  
- The inverse sine function \( \sin^{-1} x \) gives the angle in \( \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] \) whose sine is \( x \).
- The inverse cosine function \( \cos^{-1} x \) gives the angle in \( [0, \pi] \) whose cosine is \( x \).

Combining these inverse functions corresponds to combining angles in their principal branches, which geometrically represent points on the unit circle within the defined ranges.

---

**Key Theorems and Identities**

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x &= \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad -1 \leq x \leq 1 \\
\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y &= \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x + y}{1 - xy} \right), \quad xy < 1 \\
\sin(\sin^{-1} x) &= x, \quad |x| \leq 1 \\
\cos(\cos^{-1} x) &= x, \quad |x| \leq 1
\end{aligned}
}
\]

---

**Proof Sketch for \( \sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2} \):**

**Step 1:** Let \( \theta = \sin^{-1} x \), so that \( \sin \theta = x \), with \( \theta \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] \).

**Step 2:** Note that:
\[
\cos \left( \frac{\pi}{2} - \theta \right) = \sin \theta = x.
\]

**Step 3:** Since \( \cos^{-1} x \) is defined as the unique \( \phi \in [0, \pi] \) with \( \cos \phi = x \), it follows that:
\[
\cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2} - \theta = \frac{\pi}{2} - \sin^{-1} x.
\]

Therefore:
\[
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]

---

**Formulas Combining Inverse Trigonometric Functions:**

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x &= \frac{\pi}{2} \\
\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y &= \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x + y}{1 - xy} \right), \quad xy < 1 \\
\tan^{-1} x - \tan^{-1} y &= \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x - y}{1 + xy} \right), \quad xy > -1
\end{aligned}
\]

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm for Combining Inverse Trigonometric Functions):**

1. Identify which inverse functions are combined — note domains and principal ranges.

2. For sums of inverse tangents:
   - Use the tangent addition formula for angles to write \( \tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x + y}{1 - xy} \right) \) where applicable.

3. Express each inverse function in terms of an angle, apply addition or subtraction of angles, then simplify and find the corresponding inverse trigonometric value.

4. Always verify domain restrictions to ensure the principal value is correct.

---

**Graded Solved Examples:**

**Type 1:**  
Evaluate \( \sin^{-1} \frac{1}{2} + \cos^{-1} \frac{1}{2} \).

*Solution:*  
Step 1: Use identity:
\[
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]  
Step 2: Substitute \( x = \frac{1}{2} \):
\[
\sin^{-1} \frac{1}{2} + \cos^{-1} \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]

---

**Type 2:**  
Find \( \tan^{-1} 1 + \tan^{-1} 2 \).

*Solution:*  
Step 1: The formula for sum of inverse tangents:
\[
\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x + y}{1 - xy} \right), \quad \text{if } xy < 1.
\]

Step 2: Check product:
\[
xy = 1 \times 2 = 2 \not< 1,
\]
so formula does not apply directly.

Step 3: Note:
\[
\tan^{-1} 1 = \frac{\pi}{4}.
\]

Estimate \( \tan^{-1} 2 \approx 1.107 \) radians.

Step 4: Sum:
\[
\frac{\pi}{4} + 1.107 \approx 1.893~\text{radians}.
\]

Step 5: Alternatively, use complementary formula applicable when \( xy > 1 \):
\[
\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y = \pi - \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{x + y}{xy - 1} \right), \quad xy > 1.
\]

Apply it:
\[
\tan^{-1} 1 + \tan^{-1} 2 = \pi - \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{1 + 2}{2 - 1} \right) = \pi - \tan^{-1} 3,
\]
confirming the value.

---

**Type 3 (HOTS):**  
Simplify:
\[
\tan^{-1} \frac{1}{3} + \tan^{-1} \frac{1}{7}.
\]

*Solution:*  
Step 1: Check product:
\[
\frac{1}{3} \times \frac{1}{7} = \frac{1}{21} < 1.
\]

Step 2: Use sum formula:
\[
\tan^{-1} \frac{1}{3} + \tan^{-1} \frac{1}{7} = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{7}}{1 - \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{7}} \right) = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{\frac{7 + 3}{21}}{1 - \frac{1}{21}} \right) = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{\frac{10}{21}}{\frac{20}{21}} \right) = \tan^{-1} \frac{1}{2}.
\]
