Calculus often requires differentiation of inverse functions. Inverse trigonometric functions, which map from real values back to angles, are no exception. Their derivatives express how rapidly the angle changes with its trigonometric ratio. This section introduces the derivatives of inverse sine, cosine, tangent, secant, cosecant, and cotangent functions with rigorous proofs, domain considerations, and application-oriented examples.

### 2.6.1 Derivative of \( \sin^{-1} x \) and Domain Consideration

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1xVBAOrTRE74Ox3kkKnq6iuy63R7F40xe" alt="Graph of arcsin function and associated right triangle" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.6.2:</b> Inverse sine function \(y = \sin^{-1}(x)\) and related right triangle illustrating geometric interpretation</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**

The **inverse sine function** \( y = \sin^{-1}(x) \) (also written as \( y = \arcsin(x) \)) is defined as the unique angle \( y \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] \) such that
\[
\sin y = x, \quad x \in [-1, 1].
\]

**Geometric Meaning:**

The graph of \( y = \sin^{-1} x \) is the reflection of sine’s graph (restricted to \( \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] \)) about the line \( y = x \). For each \( x \) in the interval, \( \sin^{-1} x \) returns the angle whose sine is \( x \).

---

**Theorem 2.6.1:**  
**Derivative of \( y = \sin^{-1} x \):**  
\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}, \quad -1 < x < 1.
}
\]

**Proof Using Implicit Differentiation:**

**Step 1:** Set \( y = \sin^{-1} x \). Then by definition,
\[
x = \sin y, \quad y \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right].
\]

**Step 2:** Differentiate implicitly with respect to \( x \):
\[
\frac{d}{dx} x = \frac{d}{dx} (\sin y) \implies 1 = \cos y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \quad \text{(Chain rule)}.
\]

**Step 3:** Solve for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \):
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\cos y}.
\]

**Step 4:** Express \( \cos y \) in terms of \( x \). Using the Pythagorean identity:
\[
\cos^2 y = 1 - \sin^2 y = 1 - x^2.
\]

Since \( y \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] \), \( \cos y \geq 0 \), so
\[
\cos y = \sqrt{1 - x^2}.
\]

**Step 5:** Substitute back:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}.
\]

**Domain Note:** The derivative exists only for \( x \in (-1, 1) \) since the denominator must be nonzero and the square root defined.

---

**Formulas for \( y = \sin^{-1} x \):**

\[
\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \quad \text{with} \quad -1 < x < 1.
\]

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm) for Differentiating \( \sin^{-1} g(x) \):**

1. Identify the composite function argument \( g(x) \) inside \( \sin^{-1}(\cdot) \).

2. Use the chain rule:
   \[
   \frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} g(x) = \frac{g'(x)}{\sqrt{1 - (g(x))^2}}.
   \]

3. Confirm the domain: \( |g(x)| < 1 \) for the derivative to exist.

4. Simplify the expression carefully, especially the denominator.

---

**Examples:**

**Example 1 (Basic):** Find \( \frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} x \).

**Solution:**

Step 1: Use the formula directly:
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}}.
\]

Step 2: Domain is \( -1 < x < 1 \).

---

**Example 2 (Conceptual):** Differentiate \( y = \sin^{-1} \left( \frac{2x}{1+x^2} \right) \).

**Solution:**

Step 1: Let \( g(x) = \frac{2x}{1+x^2} \).

Step 2: Compute \( g'(x) \):
\[
g'(x) = \frac{2(1 + x^2) - 2x \cdot 2x}{(1+x^2)^2} = \frac{2 + 2x^2 - 4x^2}{(1+x^2)^2} = \frac{2 - 2x^2}{(1+x^2)^2} = \frac{2(1 - x^2)}{(1+x^2)^2}.
\]

Step 3: Use the chain rule:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{g'(x)}{\sqrt{1 - (g(x))^2}}.
\]

Step 4: Compute denominator:

Note that 
\[
(g(x))^2 = \left(\frac{2x}{1+x^2}\right)^2 = \frac{4x^2}{(1+x^2)^2}.
\]

So,
\[
1 - (g(x))^2 = \frac{(1+x^2)^2 - 4x^2}{(1+x^2)^2} = \frac{(1 - x^2)^2}{(1+x^2)^2}.
\]

Step 5: Substitute back:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\frac{2(1 - x^{2})}{(1+x^{2})^{2}}}{\sqrt{\frac{(1 - x^{2})^{2}}{(1+x^{2})^{2}}}} = \frac{2(1 - x^{2})/(1+x^{2})^{2}}{ \frac{|1 - x^{2}|}{1 + x^{2}} }.
\]

Since denominator involves absolute value, consider domain carefully. For \( |x| < 1 \), \( 1 - x^{2} > 0 \), so:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2(1 - x^{2})}{(1+x^{2})^{2}} \cdot \frac{1+x^{2}}{1 - x^{2}} = \frac{2}{1 + x^{2}}.
\]

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):** Prove that
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} \sqrt{x} = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x} \sqrt{1 - x}}, \quad 0 < x < 1.
\]

**Solution:**

Step 1: Let \( y = \sin^{-1} \sqrt{x} \).

Step 2: Write derivative using chain rule:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} u, \quad u = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}.
\]

Step 3: Apply formula:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{u'}{\sqrt{1 - u^{2}}}.
\]

Step 4: Compute \( u' \):
\[
u' = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x}}.
\]

Step 5: Substitute into derivative formula:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x}}}{\sqrt{1 - x}} = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x} \sqrt{1 - x}}.
\]

### 2.6.2 Derivative of \( \cos^{-1} x \)

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1JlA1___MxOZF7viSotA5TnvFkDj1_SPW" alt="Graph of arccos function and right triangle" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.6.3:</b> The inverse cosine function \( y=\cos^{-1} x \) and geometric interpretation</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**

The **inverse cosine function** \( y = \cos^{-1} x \) is the unique angle \( y \in [0, \pi] \) such that
\[
\cos y = x, \quad x \in [-1, 1].
\]

**Geometric Meaning:**

It returns the angle in radians whose cosine is \( x \), with output angles in the interval \( [0, \pi] \).

---

**Theorem 2.6.2:**  
**Derivative of \( y = \cos^{-1} x \):**
\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \cos^{-1} x = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}}, \quad -1 < x < 1.
}
\]

**Proof (Implicit Differentiation):**

**Step 1:** Write
\[
x = \cos y, \quad y \in [0, \pi].
\]

**Step 2:** Differentiate with respect to \( x \):
\[
1 = - \sin y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}.
\]

**Step 3:** Solve for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \):
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = - \frac{1}{\sin y}.
\]

**Step 4:** Express \( \sin y \) in terms of \( x \):
\[
\sin^{2} y = 1 - \cos^{2} y = 1 - x^{2}.
\]

Since \( y \in [0, \pi] \), \( \sin y \geq 0 \), so
\[
\sin y = \sqrt{1 - x^{2}}.
\]

**Step 5:** Substitute:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}}.
\]

---

**Formula:**

\[
\frac{d}{dx} \cos^{-1} x = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}}, \quad -1 < x < 1.
\]

---

**Working Rule:**

The derivative of \( \cos^{-1} g(x) \) is:
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \cos^{-1} g(x) = - \frac{g'(x)}{\sqrt{1 - (g(x))^{2}}}.
\]

Apply chain rule and domain restrictions as with \( \sin^{-1} x \).

---

**Examples:**

**Example 4 (Basic):** Differentiate \( y = \cos^{-1} \left( \frac{x}{2} \right) \).

**Solution:**

Step 1: Let \( g(x) = \frac{x}{2} \Rightarrow g'(x) = \frac{1}{2}. \)

Step 2: Apply formula:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = - \frac{g'(x)}{\sqrt{1 - (g(x))^{2}}} = - \frac{\frac{1}{2}}{\sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^2}} = - \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{1 - \frac{x^{2}}{4}}} = - \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{\frac{4 - x^{2}}{4}}} = - \frac{1}{\sqrt{4 - x^{2}}}.
\]

---

### 2.6.3 Derivative of \( \tan^{-1} x \)

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1KUFN5sbfRZ3Q4YcDYlkafhPQT7m5ccxH" alt="Graph of arctan function showing asymptotes" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.6.4:</b> Graph of \( y = \tan^{-1} x \) with horizontal asymptotes at \( \pm \frac{\pi}{2} \)</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**

The **inverse tangent function** \( y = \tan^{-1} x \) gives the angle \( y \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right) \) such that
\[
\tan y = x, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}.
\]

**Geometric Meaning:**

It converts a real number \( x \) representing a slope to the corresponding angle \( y \).

---

**Theorem 2.6.3:**  
**Derivative of \( y = \tan^{-1} x \):**
\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \tan^{-1} x = \frac{1}{1 + x^{2}}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}.
}
\]

**Proof (Implicit Differentiation):**

**Step 1:** Let
\[
x = \tan y, \quad y \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right).
\]

**Step 2:** Differentiate implicitly:
\[
1 = \sec^{2} y \frac{dy}{dx}.
\]

**Step 3:** Solve for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \):
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sec^{2} y} = \cos^{2} y.
\]

**Step 4:** Express \( \cos^{2} y \) in terms of \( x \). Use identity:
\[
1 + \tan^{2} y = \sec^{2} y \implies \sec^{2} y = 1 + x^{2}.
\]

Since \( \cos^{2} y = \frac{1}{\sec^{2} y} \), substitute:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{1 + x^{2}}.
\]

---

**Formula:**
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \tan^{-1} x = \frac{1}{1 + x^{2}}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}.
\]

---

**Working Rule:**

For \( y = \tan^{-1} g(x) \):
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{g'(x)}{1 + (g(x))^{2}}.
\]

---

**Examples:**

**Example 5 (Basic):** Find \( \frac{d}{dx} \tan^{-1} (3x) \).

**Solution:**

Step 1: \( g(x) = 3x \Rightarrow g'(x) = 3 \).

Step 2:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3}{1 + (3x)^2} = \frac{3}{1 + 9x^{2}}.
\]

---

### 2.6.4 Derivatives of \( \sec^{-1} x \), \( \csc^{-1} x \), and \( \cot^{-1} x \)

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1fkObDHUfegPzQqxmfzhW-HR-huVb8z3o" alt="Graphs of arcsec, arccsc and arccot functions" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.6.5:</b> Graphs of inverse secant, cosecant and cotangent functions</i></figcaption>
</figure>

---

**Definitions:**

- \( y = \sec^{-1} x \): \( y \in [0, \pi], y \neq \frac{\pi}{2} \), and \( |x| \geq 1 \), with \( \sec y = x \).

- \( y = \csc^{-1} x \): \( y \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right], y \neq 0 \), and \( \csc y = x \), with \( |x| \geq 1 \).

- \( y = \cot^{-1} x \): \( y \in (0, \pi) \), and \( \cot y = x \).

---

**Theorems:**

\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \sec^{-1} x = \frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{x^{2} - 1}}, \quad |x| > 1,
}
\]
\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \csc^{-1} x = -\frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{x^{2} - 1}}, \quad |x| > 1,
}
\]
\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \cot^{-1} x = -\frac{1}{1 + x^{2}}.
}
\]

---

**Proof for \( y = \sec^{-1} x \):**

**Step 1:** Let \( x = \sec y \) with \( |x| \geq 1 \), \( y \in [0, \pi], y \neq \frac{\pi}{2} \).

**Step 2:** Differentiate implicitly:
\[
1 = \sec y \tan y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}.
\]

**Step 3:** Solve for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \):
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sec y \tan y}.
\]

**Step 4:** Express \( \sec y \) and \( \tan y \) in terms of \( x \):

- Since \( \sec y = x \),

- Use identity:
\[
\tan^{2} y = \sec^{2} y - 1 = x^{2} - 1,
\]
so
\[
\tan y = \pm \sqrt{x^{2} - 1}.
\]

Considering the domain and sign conventions, the standard formula uses an absolute value to ensure positivity of the denominator.

**Step 5:** Substitute:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{x \cdot \sqrt{x^{2} - 1}} \times \text{sgn}(\tan y).
\]

Taking absolute value for general validity,
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{x^{2} - 1}}.
\]

---

**Proofs for \( \csc^{-1} x \) and \( \cot^{-1} x \) follow similarly by implicit differentiation:**

- For \( y = \csc^{-1} x \):
\[
x = \csc y, \quad |x| \geq 1, \quad y \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right], y \neq 0,
\]
differentiation yields
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{x^{2} - 1}}.
\]

- For \( y = \cot^{-1} x \):
\[
x = \cot y, \quad y \in (0, \pi),
\]
differentiation gives
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{1 + x^{2}}.
\]

---

**Working Rules:**

For composite functions \( g(x) \):

- 
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \sec^{-1} g(x) = \frac{g'(x)}{|g(x)| \sqrt{(g(x))^{2} - 1}}, \quad |g(x)| > 1.
\]
- 
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \csc^{-1} g(x) = - \frac{g'(x)}{|g(x)| \sqrt{(g(x))^{2} - 1}}, \quad |g(x)| > 1.
\]
- 
\[
\frac{d}{dx} \cot^{-1} g(x) = - \frac{g'(x)}{1 + (g(x))^{2}}.
\]

---

**Examples:**

**Example 6:** Differentiate \( y = \sec^{-1}(2x) \).

**Solution:**

Step 1: \( g(x) = 2x, \quad g'(x) = 2, \quad |2x| > 1 \).

Step 2:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2}{|2x| \sqrt{(2x)^2 - 1}} = \frac{2}{2 |x| \sqrt{4x^{2} - 1}} = \frac{1}{|x| \sqrt{4x^{2} - 1}}.
\]

---

**Example 7:** Differentiate \( y = \cot^{-1} \sqrt{x} \).

**Solution:**

Step 1: \( g(x) = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2} \Rightarrow g'(x) = \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x}} \).

Step 2:
\[
\frac{dy}{dx} = - \frac{\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x}}}{1 + x} = -\frac{1}{2 \sqrt{x} (1 + x)}.
\]

---

### 2.6.5 Proofs Using Implicit Differentiation

The common approach to find derivatives of inverse trigonometric functions relies on setting
\[
x = f(y),
\]
where \( f(y) \) is a trigonometric function with domain restrictions making \( f \) injective and invertible.

Differentiating implicitly,
\[
\frac{dx}{dy} = f'(y) \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{f'(y)}.
\]

Expressing \( y \) and trigonometric expressions in terms of \( x \) using Pythagorean identities and sign considerations yields the derivative formulas.

This method applies to all inverse trig derivatives discussed above.

---

### 2.6.6 Applications to Differentiability

- **Domain Restrictions:** Every inverse trigonometric function’s derivative is defined only over a subset of the real line corresponding to the domain of the original function and its derivative’s validity (e.g., denominators cannot vanish).

- **Non-Differentiability at Endpoints:** Note that for \( \sin^{-1} x \) and \( \cos^{-1} x \), the derivative tends to infinity near \( x = \pm 1 \), indicating vertical tangents and points of non-differentiability at endpoints.

- **Chain Rule Usage:** Many real problems involve compositions such as \( \sin^{-1}(g(x)) \), requiring careful application of the chain rule and verification of domain conditions.

- **Graphical Interpretation:** The derivatives measure slopes of tangent lines to inverse trig graphs, vital in physics (angles vs. ratios), engineering, and geometry.

\end{cases}
}
\]
