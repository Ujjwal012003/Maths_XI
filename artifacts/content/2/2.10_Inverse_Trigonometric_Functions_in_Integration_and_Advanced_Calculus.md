Integration involving inverse trigonometric functions is a vital topic in advanced calculus. These functions often emerge when integrating expressions containing radicals or rational functions, through a deep connection with their derivatives. This section will explore how derivatives of inverse trigonometric functions enable integration, discuss integral formulas involving these functions with proofs, and present graded examples to build conceptual clarity and mastery.

### 2.10.1 Using Derivatives for Integration

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1V5aXzEfPSRZ4crStQFUCQRvq8GslLcmx" alt="Derivatives of Inverse Trig Functions Conceptual Graph" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.10.2:</b> Graph illustrating the derivatives of inverse trigonometric functions and how they relate to integrals</i></figcaption>
</figure>

The crucial link between derivatives and integrals is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus which states that integration may be performed if one recognizes the integrand as the derivative of a known function. In particular, derivatives of inverse trigonometric functions give rise to integrands that often resist simpler algebraic integration, hence guiding us to integrals of inverse trig functions.

**Important Derivatives of Inverse Trigonometric Functions:**

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
&\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \quad (-1 < x < 1), \\
&\frac{d}{dx} \cos^{-1}(x) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \quad (-1 < x < 1), \\
&\frac{d}{dx} \tan^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{1 + x^2} \quad (x \in \mathbb{R}).
\end{aligned}
}
\]

**Proof of Derivative of** \( \sin^{-1}(x) \):

- **Step 1:** Let \( y = \sin^{-1}(x) \Rightarrow x = \sin y \).  
- **Step 2:** Differentiate both sides w.r.t. \( x \):
  \[
  \frac{dx}{dy} = \cos y \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\cos y}.
  \]
- **Step 3:** Using the Pythagorean identity,
  \[
  \cos y = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 y} = \sqrt{1 - x^2}.
  \]
- **Step 4:** Hence,
  \[
  \frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}.
  \]

Similarly, the derivatives for \( \cos^{-1} x \) and \( \tan^{-1} x \) follow via analogous substitutions and identities.

### 2.10.2 Integral Formulas Involving Inverse Trigonometric Functions

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1sXrPZQ3orJQYNuZhPE5K58HcWdcd_RC7" alt="Inverse Trigonometric Integral Formulas" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.10.3:</b> Relationships between inverse trig function derivatives and typical integral forms</i></figcaption>

**Standard Integral Formulas:**

These integrate functions whose derivatives correspond to inverse trigonometric functions:

\[
\int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = \sin^{-1}(x) + C,
\]

\[
\int \frac{dx}{1 + x^2} = \tan^{-1}(x) + C,
\]

\[
\int \frac{dx}{|x|\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} = \sec^{-1}(x) + C, \quad |x| > 1.
\]

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm) to Evaluate Integrals Involving Inverse Trigonometric Functions:**

1. **Step 1:** Identify if the integrand matches the derivative form of an inverse trig function or can be transformed into one.  
2. **Step 2:** Use substitution to rewrite the integrand, such as \( x = \sin \theta \), \( x = \tan \theta \), or \( x = \sec \theta \), accordingly.  
3. **Step 3:** Simplify the integral to a basic integral form or directly recognize it as the derivative of an inverse trig function.  
4. **Step 4:** Integrate using the inverse trigonometric integral formulas.  
5. **Step 5:** Resubstitute back to \( x \) if a substitution was made.  
6. **Step 6:** Add the constant of integration \( +C \).

### 2.10.3 Examples of Integration Where Inverse Trigonometric Functions Arise

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1vPLI_yHrHoA7rAXbTzJ71veqTyMLx8m3" alt="Examples involving Inverse Trig Functions in Integration" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">

**Example 1 (Concept Building):**

Evaluate
\[
\int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx.
\]

**Solution:**

- Step 1: Recognize that \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \) is the derivative of \( \sin^{-1}(x) \).  
- Step 2: Directly write,
\[
\int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = \sin^{-1}(x) + C.
\]

---

**Example 2 (Competency Based):**

Evaluate
\[
\int \frac{dx}{1 + x^2}.
\]

**Solution:**

- Step 1: Recognize this as the derivative of \( \tan^{-1}(x) \).  
- Step 2: Applying the formula,
\[
\int \frac{dx}{1 + x^2} = \tan^{-1}(x) + C.
\]

---

**Example 3 (HOTS - Higher Order Thinking Skills):**

Evaluate
\[
\int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx.
\]

**Solution:**

- Step 1: Rewrite \( x^2 \) as \( 1 - (1 - x^2) \):
\[
\int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \int \frac{1 - (1 - x^2)}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx - \int \frac{1 - x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx.
\]
- Step 2: Simplify the second integral:
\[
\int \frac{1 - x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \int \sqrt{1 - x^2} \, dx.
\]
- Step 3: Thus,
\[
\int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} - \int \sqrt{1 - x^2} \, dx.
\]
- Step 4: The first integral is \( \sin^{-1}(x) + C_1 \). For the second integral, use substitution \( x = \sin \theta \Rightarrow dx = \cos \theta \, d\theta \):
\[
\int \sqrt{1 - x^2} \, dx = \int \cos^2 \theta \, d\theta = \int \frac{1 + \cos 2\theta}{2} \, d\theta = \frac{\theta}{2} + \frac{\sin 2\theta}{4} + C_2.
\]
- Step 5: Back-substitute:
\[
\theta = \sin^{-1} x, \quad \sin 2\theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta = 2x \sqrt{1 - x^2}.
\]
So the second integral equals
\[
\frac{1}{2} \sin^{-1}(x) + \frac{x \sqrt{1 - x^2}}{2} + C_2.
\]
- Step 6: Putting it all together:
\[
\int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \sin^{-1}(x) - \left(\frac{1}{2} \sin^{-1}(x) + \frac{x \sqrt{1 - x^2}}{2}\right) + C,
\]
which simplifies as
\[
\int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \sin^{-1}(x) - \frac{x \sqrt{1 - x^2}}{2} + C.
\]

### 2.10.4 Linking Inverse Trigonometric Functions with Definite and Indefinite Integrals

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1St9BnWO5ib6Qq8MbIDjRLGqVhhuBqzuZ" alt="Definite Integral Graph linking Inverse Trigonometric Functions" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.10.5:</b> Geometric interpretation of definite integrals involving inverse trigonometric functions</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Geometric Meaning:**

Inverse trigonometric functions often represent the angle associated with a particular geometric configuration (e.g., \(\sin^{-1} x\) is the angle whose sine is \(x\)). When we compute definite integrals involving inverse trig functions, such as

\[
\int_a^b \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = \sin^{-1}(b) - \sin^{-1}(a),
\]

it corresponds geometrically to the difference of angles subtended or to the length of arcs in a unit circle segment.

**Properties:**

- Definite integrals involving integrands with radicals like \( \sqrt{1 - x^2} \) can be evaluated via inverse trigonometric substitutions reducing the integral to evaluating these angles.
- Indefinite integrals yield an infinite family of antiderivatives differing by constants but maintain the same fundamental geometric interpretation.

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm) to Solve Definite Integrals Involving Inverse Trigonometric Functions:**

1. **Step 1:** Express or recognize the integrand as a derivative of an inverse trigonometric function or transform it via substitution.  
2. **Step 2:** Apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus:
   \[
   \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a),
   \]
   where \( F'(x) = f(x) \).  
3. **Step 3:** Substitute the limits into the inverse trig function obtained as the antiderivative.  
4. **Step 4:** Simplify the difference of inverse trig functions.  
5. **Step 5:** Interpret geometrically, where possible, using unit circle or triangle relationships to check consistency.

---

## Summary of Key Formulas

\[
\begin{cases}
\displaystyle \int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = \sin^{-1}(x) + C, & -1 < x < 1, \\[10pt]
\displaystyle \int \frac{dx}{1 + x^2} = \tan^{-1}(x) + C, & x \in \mathbb{R}, \\[10pt]
\displaystyle \int \frac{dx}{|x| \sqrt{x^2 - 1}} = \sec^{-1}(x) + C, & |x| > 1.
\end{cases}
\]
