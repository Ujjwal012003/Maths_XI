### 2.5.1 Graph of $\sin^{-1} x$ (Inverse Sine Function)

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Graph of Inverse Sine Function sin^-1 x" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.5.1:</b> Graph of $y = \sin^{-1} x$ with domain $[-1,1]$ and range $\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**  
The inverse sine function, denoted as $y = \sin^{-1} x$ or $y = \arcsin x$, assigns to each $x$ in the domain $[-1,1]$ the unique angle $y$ in the interval $\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$ such that:
$$
\sin y = x \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y = \sin^{-1} x.
$$

**Geometric Meaning:**  
For a given real number $x$ with $|x| \leq 1$, $\sin^{-1} x$ returns the angle in radians whose sine value equals $x$. On the unit circle, this angle lies between $-\frac{\pi}{2}$ and $\frac{\pi}{2}$.

---

**Theorem 1 (Fundamental Property):**
\[
\boxed{
\sin(\sin^{-1} x) = x \quad \text{for } x \in [-1,1].
}
\]

**Proof:**

- *Step 1:* Let $y = \sin^{-1} x$. By definition, $y$ lies in $\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$ and satisfies $x = \sin y$.
- *Step 2:* Applying sine on both sides:
$$
\sin(\sin^{-1} x) = \sin y = x,
$$
which establishes the property.

---

**Key Formulas:**
$$
\frac{d}{dx} \sin^{-1} x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \quad \text{for } -1 < x < 1,
$$

$$
\int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} \, dx = \sin^{-1} x + C.
$$

---

**Working Rule to Solve Problems Involving $\sin^{-1} x$:**

1. Verify that the given $x$ lies within $[-1,1]$ since $\sin^{-1} x$ is only defined there.
2. Express the problem in terms of an angle whose sine equals $x$.
3. Use the principal range $\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$ for outputs.
4. Apply formulas for differentiation or integration as required.
5. Check for special cases such as $x = \pm 1$, where $\sin^{-1}(\pm 1) = \pm \frac{\pi}{2}$.

---

**Examples**

**Example 1 (Concept Building):** Find $\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)$.

*Solution:*

- Step 1: Recognize that $\sin \frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$.
- Step 2: Since $\frac{\pi}{6} \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$, the principal value is:
$$
\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{\pi}{6}.
$$

---

**Example 2 (Competency Based):** Differentiate $y = \sin^{-1}(3x)$.

*Solution:*

- Step 1: Use the chain rule and derivative formula:
$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (3x)^2}} \times 3 = \frac{3}{\sqrt{1 - 9x^{2}}}.
$$

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):** Solve for $x$ if $\sin^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} \sqrt{1-x^2} = \frac{\pi}{2}$.

*Solution:*

- Step 1: Recognize that $\sin^{-1} \sqrt{1-x^2} = \cos^{-1} x$ since the inverse sine and cosine satisfy the identity:
$$
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$
- Step 2: Hence,
$$
\sin^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} \sqrt{1-x^2} = \sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$
- Step 3: This holds for every $x \in [-1,1]$; therefore, all $x$ in the domain satisfy the equation.

### 2.5.2 Graph of $\cos^{-1} x$ (Inverse Cosine Function)

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Graph of Inverse Cosine Function cos^-1 x" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.5.2:</b> Graph of $y = \cos^{-1} x$ with domain $[-1, 1]$ and range $[0, \pi]$</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**  
The inverse cosine function $\cos^{-1} x$ is defined for $x \in [-1,1]$ as the unique angle $y$ in $[0, \pi]$ such that:
$$
\cos y = x \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y = \cos^{-1} x.
$$

**Geometric Meaning:**  
$\cos^{-1} x$ gives the angle whose cosine equals $x$ in the principal range $[0, \pi]$, often visualized on the unit circle as angles on the upper half semicircle.

---

**Theorem 2 (Derivative of $\cos^{-1} x$):**
\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \cos^{-1} x = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}} \quad \text{for } -1 < x < 1.
}
\]

**Proof:**

- Step 1: Let $y = \cos^{-1} x$ so that $\cos y = x$ with $y \in [0, \pi]$.

- Step 2: Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$:
$$
-\sin y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{\sin y}.
$$

- Step 3: Using the identity $\sin^2 y = 1 - \cos^2 y = 1 - x^{2}$, we have:
$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}}.
$$

---

**Key Formulas:**
$$
\int \cos^{-1} x \, dx = x \cos^{-1} x - \sqrt{1 - x^{2}} + C.
$$

---

**Working Rule to Solve Problems Involving $\cos^{-1} x$:**

1. Confirm the domain $x \in [-1,1]$.

2. Use principal range $ y \in [0, \pi]$.

3. Use derivative and integral formulas for calculus problems.

4. Recall identities involving $\cos^{-1} x$, for example,
$$
\cos^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

---

**Examples**

**Example 1 (Concept Building):** Find $\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)$.

*Solution:*

- Step 1: Since $\cos \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\frac{\pi}{3} \in [0, \pi]$,
$$
\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{\pi}{3}.
$$

---

**Example 2 (Competency Based):** Compute $\frac{d}{dx} \left[ \cos^{-1}(2x) \right]$.

*Solution:*

- Step 1: Use chain rule:
$$
\frac{d}{dx} \cos^{-1}(2x) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - (2x)^2}} \times 2 = -\frac{2}{\sqrt{1 - 4x^{2}}}.
$$

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):** Evaluate $\displaystyle \int \frac{\cos^{-1} x}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}} \, dx$.

*Solution:*

- Step 1: Use substitution $t = \cos^{-1} x$, then $dt = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}} dx$, thus:
$$
-\!dt = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}} dx.
$$

- Step 2: Rewrite integral:
$$
\int \frac{\cos^{-1} x}{\sqrt{1 - x^{2}}} dx = - \int t \, dt = -\frac{t^{2}}{2} + C = -\frac{(\cos^{-1} x)^2}{2} + C.
$$

### 2.5.3 Graph of $\tan^{-1} x$ (Inverse Tangent Function)

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Graph of Inverse Tangent Function tan^-1 x" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.5.3:</b> Graph of $y = \tan^{-1} x$ with domain $(-\infty, \infty)$ and range $\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$ depicting horizontal asymptotes</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**  
The inverse tangent function $y = \tan^{-1} x$ (or $\arctan x$) is defined for all real numbers $x$, giving the unique angle $y$ in the interval $\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$ such that:
$$
\tan y = x.
$$

**Geometric Meaning:**  
$\tan^{-1} x$ gives the angle between $-\frac{\pi}{2}$ and $\frac{\pi}{2}$ whose tangent is $x$. The graph grows continuously, approaching but never reaching the horizontal asymptotes $y = \pm \frac{\pi}{2}$.

---

**Theorem 3 (Derivative and Monotonicity):**
\[
\boxed{
\frac{d}{dx} \tan^{-1} x = \frac{1}{1+x^2}, \quad \text{and } \tan^{-1} x \text{ is strictly increasing on } \mathbb{R}.
}
\]

**Proof:**

- Step 1: Let $y = \tan^{-1} x$, so $\tan y = x$ with $y \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$.

- Step 2: Differentiate implicitly:
$$
\sec^{2} y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sec^{2} y} = \cos^{2} y.
$$

- Step 3: Using identity $\tan^{2} y + 1 = \sec^{2} y$, and substituting $x = \tan y$, obtain:
$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{1 + x^{2}} > 0,
$$
confirming the function is strictly increasing on $\mathbb{R}$.

---

**Important Asymptotes:**

- Horizontal asymptotes:
$$
y = \frac{\pi}{2} \quad \text{as } x \to +\infty,
$$
$$
y = -\frac{\pi}{2} \quad \text{as } x \to -\infty.
$$

---

**Key Formulas:**
$$
\int \tan^{-1} x \, dx = x \tan^{-1} x - \frac{1}{2} \ln(1 + x^{2}) + C.
$$

---

**Working Rule:**

1. Recognize domain as all real numbers.

2. Use derivative formula $\frac{1}{1 + x^{2}}$ and horizontal asymptotes in analyses.

3. For limits, understand the approach to $\pm \frac{\pi}{2}$.

---

**Examples**

**Example 1 (Concept Building):** Find $\tan^{-1} 1$.

*Solution:*

- Since $\tan \frac{\pi}{4} = 1$, the principal value is:
$$
\tan^{-1} 1 = \frac{\pi}{4}.
$$

---

**Example 2 (Competency Based):** Differentiate $y = \tan^{-1}(2x)$.

*Solution:*

- Step 1: Apply chain rule:
$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{1 + (2x)^2} \times 2 = \frac{2}{1 + 4x^{2}}.
$$

---

**Example 3 (HOTS):** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} \tan^{-1} x$.

*Solution:*

- As $x \to \infty$,
$$
\tan^{-1} x \to \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

### 2.5.4 Reflection Principle About $y = x$

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Reflection Principle about y equals x" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.5.4:</b> Graphs of a function and its inverse reflected about the line $y = x$</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Statement:**  
*For a bijective function $f$ and its inverse $f^{-1}$, the graph of $f^{-1}$ is the reflection of the graph of $f$ about the line $y = x$.*

---

**Proof:**

- Let $(a,b)$ be any point on the graph of $f$, so $b = f(a)$.

- By definition of inverse,
$$
a = f^{-1}(b).
$$

- Therefore, the point $(b,a)$ lies on the graph of $f^{-1}$.

- Geometrically, reflecting the point $(a,b)$ about the line $y = x$ maps it to $(b,a)$.

- Since this holds for every point $(a,b)$ on $f$, the graph of $f^{-1}$ is the mirror image of the graph of $f$ about $y = x$.

---

**Implication for Inverse Trig Functions:**

The graphs of inverse trigonometric functions (e.g., $y = \sin^{-1} x$) can be understood as reflections of their original functions (e.g., $y = \sin x$ restricted to $\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$) about the line $y = x$.

### 2.5.5 Geometric Interpretation Using Right Triangles

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Right Triangle Interpretation of Inverse Trigonometric Functions" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.5.5:</b> Using right triangles to interpret inverse trigonometric functions</i></figcaption>
</figure>

Inverse trigonometric functions can be interpreted as angle measures corresponding to specific side ratios in right triangles:

- Consider a right triangle with opposite side length $a$, adjacent side length $b$, and hypotenuse length $c$.

| Function          | Expression                         | Geometric Interpretation                     |
|-------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| $\sin^{-1} x$     | $\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{a}{c}\right)$ | Angle whose sine is $\frac{a}{c}$              |
| $\cos^{-1} x$     | $\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{b}{c}\right)$ | Angle whose cosine is $\frac{b}{c}$            |
| $\tan^{-1} x$     | $\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)$ | Angle whose tangent is $\frac{a}{b}$           |

---

**Example Using a 3-4-5 Triangle:**

- Opposite side = 3, adjacent side = 4, hypotenuse = 5.

- Calculate:
$$
\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{3}{5}\right), \quad \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{4}{5}\right), \quad \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right).
$$

They provide the angle measures of the right triangle, showing direct geometric correspondence.
