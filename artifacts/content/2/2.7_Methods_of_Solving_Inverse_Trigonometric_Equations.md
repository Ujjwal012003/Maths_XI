## 2.7 Methods of Solving Inverse Trigonometric Equations

Inverse trigonometric equations involve finding the angle whose trigonometric ratio is given. Unlike regular trigonometric equations, special care must be taken due to the **principal value restrictions** of inverse trig functions. The methods covered here combine algebraic skill, understanding domain restrictions, and applying identities.

---

### 2.7.1 Basic Solving Techniques

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Basic inverse trig equation solution illustration" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.7.2:</b> Solving basic inverse trigonometric equations graphically</i></figcaption>
</figure>

**Definition:**

An **inverse trigonometric equation** is an equation where the unknown appears as an input to an inverse trigonometric function, e.g., $\sin^{-1}x = \alpha$ or $\tan^{-1}y = \beta$.

**Geometric Meaning:**  
These correspond to points on the graph of the inverse trig function, where solving the equation means finding the $x$-values giving the specified $y$-value (angle).

---

**Theorem (Principal Value Definition):**  
Each inverse trig function has a unique principal value range ensuring it is a proper function:

$$
\begin{cases}
\sin^{-1} x \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]\\[6pt]
\cos^{-1} x \in [0, \pi]\\[6pt]
\tan^{-1} x \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)
\end{cases}
$$

---

**Step-by-Step Proof for Unique Solutions:**

1. Each trig function is **many-to-one** on $\mathbb{R}$. To define its inverse, restrict its domain (e.g., restrict sine to $[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}]$) so that it becomes one-to-one.

2. The restricted domain is chosen so that the inverse functions become continuous and monotonic, guaranteeing uniqueness of the inverse images.

3. Within these principal branches, inverse function values are uniquely defined.

---

**Formulas:**

Key identities useful at this level include:

$$
\sin(\sin^{-1} x) = x, \quad x \in [-1,1]
$$

$$
\cos(\cos^{-1} x) = x, \quad x \in [-1,1]
$$

$$
\tan(\tan^{-1} x) = x, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}
$$

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm) to Solve Basic Equations:**

1. Identify which inverse trig function is involved.

2. Write the equation in the form:
   $$
   \text{inverse trig function} = \text{some constant or expression}
   $$

3. Use the property of inverse functions to write the equivalent trig form:
   $$
   \sin^{-1} x = \alpha \implies x = \sin \alpha
   $$

4. Check the domain of the inverse function to ensure $\alpha$ lies in the principal range.

5. Find all possible solutions keeping within the domain and range restrictions.

---

**Example 1 (Basic):**

Solve for $x$:

$$
\sin^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{6}
$$

**Solution:**

- Step 1: By the definition of inverse sine,
$$
x = \sin \frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{1}{2}
$$

- Step 2: Check the domain of $\sin^{-1} x$:
$$
x \in [-1,1], \quad \frac{1}{2} \in [-1,1]
$$

- Step 3: Therefore, the solution is:
$$
\boxed{x = \frac{1}{2}}
$$

### 2.7.2 Using Domain and Range Restrictions

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Domain and range explanation" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.7.3:</b> Domain and range restrictions of inverse trig functions</i></figcaption>
</figure>

---

**Definition:**

The domain and range restrictions of inverse trig functions control the valid inputs and outputs of these functions.

**Geometric Meaning:**

On the graph of an inverse trig function, only the segment reflecting the principal branch is considered.

---

**Theorem (Domain and Range Restrictions):**

For an inverse trigonometric function $f^{-1}$,

$$
\text{Domain}(f^{-1}) = \text{Range}(f), \quad \text{Range}(f^{-1}) = \text{Domain}(f)|_{\text{principal branch}}
$$

Explicitly,

- For $y = \sin^{-1} x$:
$$
x \in [-1,1], \quad y \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]
$$

- For $y = \cos^{-1} x$:
$$
x \in [-1,1], \quad y \in [0, \pi]
$$

- For $y = \tan^{-1} x$:
$$
x \in \mathbb{R}, \quad y \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)
$$

---

**Step-by-Step Reasoning:**

1. Verify the argument inside the inverse function lies within its domain; for example, for $\sin^{-1} x$, $x$ must be in $[-1,1]$.

2. Ensure the resulting angle is within the principal range, else consider other solution branches by using periodicity of the original trig functions.

3. Sometimes, the equation must be restricted or solved within a given interval for $x$ or the angle.

---

**Formulas to Note:**

- For $y = \sin^{-1} x$,
$$
x = \sin y, \quad y \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]
$$

- For $y = \cos^{-1} x$,
$$
x = \cos y, \quad y \in [0, \pi]
$$

- For $y = \tan^{-1} x$,
$$
x = \tan y, \quad y \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)
$$

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm) to Use Domain and Range Restrictions:**

1. Check if the value inside the inverse function lies in its domain.

2. Solve for the angle using direct inversion.

3. If required, consider additional solutions arising from the periodic nature (for equations, not just inverse function definition), but ensure principal values are respected.

4. Verify if the solution satisfies the domain of the variable.

---

**Example 2 (Conceptual):**

Solve for $x$:

$$
\cos^{-1} x = \frac{3\pi}{4}
$$

**Solution:**

- Step 1: The domain of $\cos^{-1} x$ is $x \in [-1,1]$ and the range is $[0, \pi]$.

- Step 2: Since $\frac{3\pi}{4} \in [0, \pi]$, proceed.

- Step 3: From definition,
$$
x = \cos \frac{3\pi}{4} = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}
$$

- Step 4: Check the domain:
$$
-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \in [-1,1]
$$

- Step 5: Therefore, the solution is:
$$
\boxed{x = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}}
$$

### 2.7.3 Applying Identities to Simplify Equations

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Using inverse trig identities" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.7.4:</b> Using inverse trig identities to transform and simplify equations</i></figcaption>
</figure>

---

**Definition:**

Inverse trigonometric identities relate sums, differences, and compositions of inverse trig functions to algebraic expressions or other inverse trigonometric functions.

**Geometric Meaning:**

These identities are derived from the geometry of triangles or the unit circle and enable expression of complicated inverse trig sums as single inverse trig expressions or algebraic terms.

---

**Important Identities (Boxed):**

\[
\begin{aligned}
\sin^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} y &= \sin^{-1}\left(x \sqrt{1 - y^2} + y \sqrt{1 - x^2}\right), \quad xy \geq 0, \quad x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \\
\sin^{-1} x - \sin^{-1} y &= \sin^{-1}\left(x \sqrt{1 - y^2} - y \sqrt{1 - x^2}\right), \quad x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \\
\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y &= \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{x + y}{1 - xy}\right), \quad xy < 1 \\
\tan^{-1} x - \tan^{-1} y &= \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{x - y}{1 + xy}\right), \quad \forall x,y
\end{aligned}
\]

---

**Proof Sketch for $\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y$ formula:**

1. Let
$$
A = \tan^{-1} x, \quad B = \tan^{-1} y,
$$
then
$$
\tan A = x, \quad \tan B = y.
$$

2. Using the angle addition formula for tangent:
$$
\tan(A + B) = \frac{\tan A + \tan B}{1 - \tan A \tan B} = \frac{x + y}{1 - xy}.
$$

3. Taking inverse tangent on both sides, considering the principal branches,
$$
A + B = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x + y}{1 - xy}\right).
$$

Therefore,
$$
\tan^{-1} x + \tan^{-1} y = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x + y}{1 - xy}\right),
$$
provided $xy < 1$ to ensure the expression is defined on the principal branch.

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm) for Applying Identities:**

1. Look for sums or differences of inverse trig functions in the equation.

2. Apply the appropriate identity to convert sums/differences into single inverse trig functions or algebraic forms.

3. Simplify the expression as far as possible.

4. Use domain restrictions to solve for unknowns.

---

**Example 3 (Competency):**

Solve for $x$:

$$
\sin^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} \sqrt{1 - x^2} = \frac{\pi}{2}
$$

**Solution:**

- Step 1: Use the identity for sum of inverse sines:
$$
\sin^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} y = \sin^{-1} \left( x \sqrt{1-y^2} + y \sqrt{1 - x^2} \right),
$$
where $y = \sqrt{1 - x^2}$.

- Step 2: Note $y^2 = 1 - x^2$, hence
$$
\sqrt{1 - y^2} = \sqrt{1 - (1 - x^2)} = \sqrt{x^2} = |x|.
$$

- Step 3: Substitute into the formula:
\[
\begin{aligned}
&\sin^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} \sqrt{1 - x^2} \\
&= \sin^{-1} \left(x |x| + \sqrt{1 - x^2} \sqrt{1 - x^2} \right) \\
&= \sin^{-1} \left(x|x| + 1 - x^2\right).
\end{aligned}
\]

- Step 4: Since $x|x| = x^2$ (because $|x| \geq 0$),
$$
x|x| + 1 - x^2 = x^2 + 1 - x^2 = 1.
$$

- Step 5: Thus,
$$
\sin^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} \sqrt{1 - x^2} = \sin^{-1} 1 = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

- Step 6: The given identity holds for $x \in [0,1]$ because $y = \sqrt{1 - x^2} \geq 0$ and the domain conditions for the identity apply.

- Step 7: Therefore,
$$
\boxed{x \in [0,1]}.
$$

### 2.7.4 Examples Involving Multiple Inverse Trig Functions

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/placeholder" alt="Equation involving multiple inverse trig functions" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.7.5:</b> Handling equations with multiple inverse trigonometric functions</i></figcaption>
</figure>

---

**Definition:**

These equations involve multiple inverse trigonometric functions combined either additively, multiplicatively, or otherwise.

---

**Working Rule (Algorithm):**

1. **Express each inverse trig function as an angle**, for example:
$$
\sin^{-1} x = A, \quad \cos^{-1} y = B, \quad \tan^{-1} z = C.
$$

2. Use **trigonometric identities** to relate these angles when they appear in sums or products, such as:
$$
A + B = \alpha, \quad \text{or} \quad \sin(A + B) = \text{value}.
$$

3. If possible, use algebraic substitutions or apply known identities (like sum and difference formulas).

4. Simplify to algebraic expressions and solve for unknown(s).

5. **Always check domain and range restrictions** for each inverse function.

---

**Example 4 (HOTS):**

Solve for $x$:

$$
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}
$$

**Solution:**

- Step 1: Let
$$
A = \sin^{-1} x, \quad B = \cos^{-1} x.
$$

- Step 2: Recall the identity:
$$
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad \text{for } x \in [-1, 1].
$$

- Step 3: This holds for all $x$ in the domain.

- Step 4: Therefore,
$$
\boxed{\text{All } x \in [-1,1] \text{ satisfy the equation}}.
$$

---

**Example 5 (HOTS):**

Solve for $x$:

$$
\tan^{-1} \frac{1 - x}{1 + x} + \tan^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{4}
$$

**Solution:**

- Step 1: Use the addition formula for inverse tangent:
$$
\tan^{-1} a + \tan^{-1} b = \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{a + b}{1 - ab}\right), \quad ab < 1.
$$

Set
$$
a = \frac{1 - x}{1 + x}, \quad b = x.
$$

- Step 2: Calculate numerator:
\[
\begin{aligned}
a + b &= \frac{1 - x}{1 + x} + x = \frac{1 - x + x(1 + x)}{1 + x} = \frac{1 - x + x + x^2}{1 + x} = \frac{1 + x^2}{1 + x}.
\end{aligned}
\]

- Step 3: Calculate denominator:
\[
\begin{aligned}
1 - ab &= 1 - \left(\frac{1 - x}{1 + x} \cdot x \right) = 1 - \frac{x (1 - x)}{1 + x} = \frac{(1 + x) - x (1 - x)}{1 + x} = \frac{1 + x - x + x^2}{1 + x} = \frac{1 + x^2}{1 + x}.
\end{aligned}
\]

- Step 4: Substitute numerator and denominator:
$$
\tan^{-1} a + \tan^{-1} b = \tan^{-1} \left(\frac{\frac{1 + x^2}{1 + x}}{\frac{1 + x^2}{1 + x}}\right) = \tan^{-1}(1) = \frac{\pi}{4}.
$$

- Step 5: The equation holds for all $x$ where the denominator is defined and the product condition $ab < 1$ holds.

- Step 6: The denominator $1 + x$ cannot be zero, so $x \neq -1$.

- Step 7: Therefore, the solution set is:
$$
\boxed{x \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{-1\}}.
$$
