Inverse trigonometric functions are the inverses of the basic trigonometric functions, defined in restricted domains to be one-to-one. Their properties and identities play a vital role in simplifying expressions and solving equations involving angles. This section covers key properties like complementary angle identities, symmetry and periodicity behavior, and inter-relations between inverse functions supported by rigorous proofs.

---

### 2.3.1 Complementary Angle Identities

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1hxX3VnLPpTvSezKuQav1hgfp6U6q_LY5" alt="Illustration of complementary angles on a unit circle" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.3.2:</b> Complementary angles $A$ and $\frac{\pi}{2}-A$ demonstrating key inverse trig identities</i></figcaption>
</figure>

#### Definition and Geometric Meaning:

For angles $A$ and $B$, if $A + B = \frac{\pi}{2}$, then $A$ and $B$ are called **complementary angles**. In inverse trigonometric functions, this manifests as relations between inverse sine and inverse cosine, inverse tangent and inverse cotangent, etc.

On the unit circle, sine of an angle equals cosine of its complement and vice versa:

$$
\sin A = \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2} - A\right).
$$

Taking inverses leads to important identities.

---

#### Theorem 1: Complementary Angle Identity for Inverse Sine and Cosine

**Statement:**

\[
\boxed{
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2} \quad \text{for}\quad x \in [-1, 1].
}
\]

---

#### Proof:

**Step 1:** Let

$$
\theta = \sin^{-1} x, \quad \text{so that} \quad \sin \theta = x, \quad \theta \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right].
$$

**Step 2:** Consider

$$
\phi = \cos^{-1} x, \quad \text{so} \quad \cos \phi = x, \quad \phi \in [0, \pi].
$$

**Step 3:** Observe that

$$
\cos \theta = \sqrt{1 - \sin^2 \theta} = \sqrt{1 - x^2}.
$$

We choose the positive root because $\theta \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$, and cosine is non-negative for $\theta \in \left[0, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]$ and negative for $\theta \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, 0\right]$; however, the following reasoning does not depend on sign because of the argument below.

**Step 4:** Since $\sin \theta = x = \cos \phi$, and we know from the unit circle that

$$
\sin \theta = \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{2} - \theta\right),
$$

it follows that the angle $\phi$ with cosine $x$ in $[0, \pi]$ equals

$$
\phi = \frac{\pi}{2} - \theta.
$$

**Step 5:** Therefore,

$$
\cos^{-1} x = \phi = \frac{\pi}{2} - \sin^{-1} x,
$$

which implies

$$
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

This completes the proof.

---

#### Theorem 2: Complementary Angle Identity for Inverse Tangent and Cotangent

**Statement:**

\[
\boxed{
\tan^{-1} x + \cot^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R},\; x > 0.
}
\]

---

#### Proof:

**Step 1:** Let

$$
\alpha = \tan^{-1} x \implies \tan \alpha = x,
$$

and

$$
\beta = \cot^{-1} x \implies \cot \beta = x.
$$

**Step 2:** Using the identity

$$
\cot \beta = \tan \left( \frac{\pi}{2} - \beta \right),
$$

we substitute to get

$$
\tan \left( \frac{\pi}{2} - \beta \right) = x = \tan \alpha.
$$

**Step 3:** Since $\tan$ is one-to-one on its principal domain $\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$, and since $\alpha, \frac{\pi}{2} - \beta$ belong to this domain (principal values), we conclude

$$
\alpha = \frac{\pi}{2} - \beta,
$$

which rearranges as

$$
\alpha + \beta = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

Hence,

$$
\tan^{-1} x + \cot^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

### 2.3.2 Symmetry and Periodicity Properties

</figure>

---

#### Definition:

- A function \(f\) is **even** if \(f(-x) = f(x)\) for all \(x\) in its domain.
- A function \(f\) is **odd** if \(f(-x) = -f(x)\) for all \(x\) in its domain.

---

#### Theorem 3: Symmetry Properties of Inverse Sine and Cosine

\[
\boxed{
\begin{cases}
\sin^{-1}(-x) = -\sin^{-1} x, \quad x \in [-1,1], \quad \text{(odd function)} \\
\cos^{-1}(-x) = \pi - \cos^{-1} x, \quad x \in [-1,1]
\end{cases}
}
\]

---

#### Proof:

**Part (a):** For the inverse sine:

**Step 1:** Let \(\theta = \sin^{-1} x\), then \(\sin \theta = x\) with \(\theta \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]\).

**Step 2:** Then

$$
\sin(-\theta) = -\sin \theta = -x.
$$

**Step 3:** Since sine is an odd function and inverse sine returns principal values in \(\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]\), it follows that

$$
\sin^{-1}(-x) = -\sin^{-1} x.
$$

---

**Part (b):** For the inverse cosine:

**Step 1:** Let \(\phi = \cos^{-1} x\), then \(\cos \phi = x\) with \(\phi \in [0,\pi]\).

**Step 2:** We note that

$$
\cos(\pi - \phi) = -\cos \phi = -x.
$$

**Step 3:** Since \(\cos^{-1}(-x)\) is the angle in \([0, \pi]\) whose cosine is \(-x\), and since \(\pi - \phi\) satisfies this, we conclude:

$$
\cos^{-1}(-x) = \pi - \cos^{-1} x.
$$

---

#### Remark on Periodicity:

While trigonometric functions are periodic, their inverse functions are **not periodic**, as their domains are restricted to make them one-to-one. However, as the above theorems show, inverse trigonometric functions exhibit **symmetry properties** which are essential for simplification.

### 2.3.3 Key Identities Among Inverse Trigonometric Functions

<figure style="display: flex; flex-direction: column; align-items: center;">
<img src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/d/1NdN3ALRXc5YONHOJGo-SJ4m-dZs1yGH3" alt="Relation circle for inverse sine and cosine" style="max-width: 90%; height: auto;">
<figcaption style="text-align: center; margin-top: 8px;"><i><b>Figure 2.3.4:</b> Unit circle interpretation of $ \sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}$</i></figcaption>
</figure>

---

#### Key Identities:

\[
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad x \in [-1,1],
\]

\[
\tan^{-1} x + \cot^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad x > 0,
\]

\[
\sec^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} \frac{1}{x} = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad |x| \geq 1,
\]

\[
\csc^{-1} x + \sin^{-1} \frac{1}{x} = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad |x| \geq 1.
\]

---

#### Interrelation Formulas:

Inverse trigonometric functions can be related by algebraic expressions using their defining relations:

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\sin^{-1} x &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\right), \quad |x| < 1, \\
\cos^{-1} x &= \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}{x}\right), \quad x \in (-1,1) \setminus \{0\}, \\
\tan^{-1} x &= \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{1 + x^2}}\right), \quad x \in \mathbb{R}, \\
\cot^{-1} x &= \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + x^2}}\right), \quad x \neq 0.
\end{aligned}
}
\]

These forms are immensely helpful in integration and solving equations involving inverse trig functions.

---

### Working Rule (Algorithm) for Solving Problems Involving these Identities

1. **Identify the inverse trigonometric functions involved** and note their domain restrictions.

2. **Recall the principal ranges** of each inverse function to ensure correct interpretation of values.

3. **Use complementary angle identities** to combine or simplify sums involving inverse trig functions. For example, replace \(\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x\) with \(\frac{\pi}{2}\).

4. **Leverage symmetry properties** for negative arguments to simplify expressions with negative inputs.

5. **Apply algebraic substitutions** using interrelation formulas to rewrite one inverse function in terms of another, aiding integration or solving equations.

6. **Confirm the solution lies in the principal value range** because inverse trigonometric functions have restricted ranges.

7. For complex problems, **break the expression into parts**, use identities stepwise, and carefully combine results.

### 2.3.4 Graded Solved Examples

---

#### Example 1 (Concept Building): Evaluate \(\sin^{-1} \frac{1}{2} + \cos^{-1} \frac{1}{2}\).

**Step 1:** Recognize from the complementary angle identity that

$$
\sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

**Step 2:** Substitute \(x = \frac{1}{2}\):

$$
\sin^{-1} \frac{1}{2} + \cos^{-1} \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

**Step 3:** *Verification:* 

\[
\sin^{-1} \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\pi}{6}, \quad \cos^{-1} \frac{1}{2} = \frac{\pi}{3}.
\]

Sum:

\[
\frac{\pi}{6} + \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]

âœ“ Identity confirmed.

---

#### Example 2 (Competency Based): Simplify the expression

\[
\tan^{-1} \left(\frac{3}{4}\right) + \cot^{-1}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right).
\]

**Step 1:** Use the identity:

\[
\tan^{-1} x + \cot^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad x > 0.
\]

Since \(\frac{3}{4} > 0\), the identity applies.

**Step 2:** Therefore,

\[
\tan^{-1} \left(\frac{3}{4}\right) + \cot^{-1} \left(\frac{3}{4}\right) = \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]

---

#### Example 3 (HOTS): Prove that

\[
2 \tan^{-1} x = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{2x}{1 + x^2}\right), \quad |x| \leq 1.
\]

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Let

\[
\theta = \tan^{-1} x, \quad \Rightarrow \quad \tan \theta = x, \quad \theta \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right).
\]

**Step 2:** Using the double-angle formula for tangent:

\[
\tan 2\theta = \frac{2 \tan \theta}{1 - \tan^2 \theta} = \frac{2x}{1 - x^2}.
\]

**Step 3:** Compute \(\sin 2\theta\) via the relation between \(\sin\) and \(\tan\):

\[
\sin 2\theta = \frac{2 \tan \theta}{1 + \tan^2 \theta} = \frac{2x}{1 + x^2}.
\]

**Step 4:** Since \(\sin (2 \theta) = \frac{2x}{1 + x^2}\) and \(2 \theta = 2 \tan^{-1} x\), we get

\[
\sin^{-1} \left( \frac{2x}{1 + x^2} \right) = 2 \tan^{-1} x.
\]

**Step 5:** Range considerations:

For \(x \in [-1,1]\), the value of \(\frac{2x}{1+x^2}\) lies in \([-1,1]\), and \(2 \tan^{-1} x\) lies in \(\left[-\pi, \pi\right]\). Principal branches align, so equality holds.

Hence proved.

---

#### Example 4 (HOTS): Find the exact value of

\[
\sin^{-1} \frac{4}{5} + \sin^{-1} \frac{12}{13}.
\]

**Step 1:** Let

\[
A = \sin^{-1} \frac{4}{5}, \quad B = \sin^{-1} \frac{12}{13}.
\]

We want to find \(A + B\).

---

**Step 2:** Calculate individual sines and cosines:

\[
\sin A = \frac{4}{5} \implies \cos A = \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^2} = \frac{3}{5}.
\]

\[
\sin B = \frac{12}{13} \implies \cos B = \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{12}{13}\right)^2} = \frac{5}{13}.
\]

---

**Step 3:** Use the sine addition formula:

\[
\sin(A + B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B = \frac{4}{5} \cdot \frac{5}{13} + \frac{3}{5} \cdot \frac{12}{13} = \frac{20}{65} + \frac{36}{65} = \frac{56}{65}.
\]

---

**Step 4:** Use the cosine addition formula:

\[
\cos(A + B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B = \frac{3}{5} \cdot \frac{5}{13} - \frac{4}{5} \cdot \frac{12}{13} = \frac{15}{65} - \frac{48}{65} = -\frac{33}{65}.
\]

---

**Step 5:** Determine the quadrant of \(A + B\):

Since \(\sin(A + B) > 0\) and \(\cos(A + B) < 0\), the angle \(A + B\) lies in the second quadrant.

Thus,

\[
A + B = \pi - \cos^{-1} \left| -\frac{33}{65} \right| = \pi - \cos^{-1} \frac{33}{65}.
\]

---

**Step 6:** Final answer:

\[
\sin^{-1} \frac{4}{5} + \sin^{-1} \frac{12}{13} = \pi - \cos^{-1} \frac{33}{65}.
\]

---

## Summary of Key Formulas

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
& \sin^{-1} x + \cos^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad x \in [-1,1], \\
& \tan^{-1} x + \cot^{-1} x = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad x > 0, \\
& \sin^{-1} (-x) = -\sin^{-1} x, \\
& \cos^{-1}(-x) = \pi - \cos^{-1} x, \\
& 2 \tan^{-1} x = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{2x}{1 + x^2}\right), \quad |x| \leq 1, \\
& \sin^{-1} x = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}}\right), \quad |x| < 1.
\end{aligned}
}
\]

---

## Final Note:

Understanding these properties and identities enables smooth manipulation of inverse trigonometric expressions. Always verify domain and range constraints before applying formulas to ensure correct principal values.
